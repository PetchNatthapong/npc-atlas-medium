1. Principles of Oncology

This section provides the foundational framework for understanding cancer biology, staging, systemic therapy principles, and supportive care — forming the bedrock upon which all subspecialty oncology knowledge rests. Content is written at mixed IM + Oncology Fellow level incorporating 2025 ESMO, ASCO, and NCCN guidelines.

1.1 Cancer Biology & Hallmarks of Cancer

▼1.1 Hallmarks of Cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg)

Hanahan and Weinberg's seminal framework describes cancer as a multi-step acquisition of biological capabilities. The 2022 update adds four emerging hallmarks.

▼❓ Practice Questions — Principles of Oncology
Q1. A 45-year-old woman with metastatic colorectal cancer has her tumor tested. The tumor is MSI-high. Which of the following statements is MOST accurate regarding her treatment?
Answer: Pembrolizumab is the preferred first-line treatment over FOLFOX/FOLFIRI
MSI-H/dMMR CRC: The KEYNOTE-177 trial demonstrated pembrolizumab monotherapy superior to chemotherapy as first-line treatment in MSI-H metastatic CRC (PFS 16.5 vs 8.2 months, ORR 43.8% vs 33.1%). MSI-H also has implications for Lynch syndrome screening (germline testing recommended), and historically a better prognosis in early-stage disease. Stage II CRC with MSI-H does NOT benefit from adjuvant 5-FU.
Q2. A patient receiving 5-FU/capecitabine-based chemotherapy develops severe diarrhea, mucositis, and myelosuppression after cycle 1, despite standard dosing. Which genetic variant is most likely responsible?
Answer: DPYD*2A variant (dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase deficiency)
DPD is the rate-limiting enzyme in 5-FU catabolism. DPYD variants (especially *2A: c.1905+1G>A splice site variant) reduce DPD activity, leading to 5-FU accumulation and severe/fatal toxicity. Management: discontinue fluoropyrimidine, supportive care, consider uridine triacetate (antidote). Pre-screening with DPYD genotyping is now recommended (EMA mandate). Complete DPD deficiency is rare (0.1%) but partial deficiency in 3-5%.
Q3. Which cancer syndrome is characterized by mucocutaneous pigmentation, GI hamartomatous polyps, and significantly increased risk of pancreatic cancer?
Answer: Peutz-Jeghers Syndrome (STK11/LKB1 mutation)
Peutz-Jeghers: autosomal dominant; STK11/LKB1 germline mutation; hallmark = perioral and buccal mucocutaneous pigmentation (fade after puberty) + GI hamartomatous polyps. Lifetime cancer risks: CRC 39%, gastric 29%, pancreatic 11-36%, breast 45-50%, ovarian (sex cord tumors), lung 15%. Pancreatic cancer risk is particularly elevated — annual pancreatic MRI/EUS from age 30. Contrast with FAP (APC gene, adenomatous polyps, 100% CRC risk) and Lynch (MMR genes, microsatellite instability).

2. Lung Cancer

Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, accounting for ~18% of cancer deaths. Treatment has been transformed by molecular profiling and immunotherapy, shifting from a histology-based to a biomarker-driven approach. This section covers NSCLC and SCLC based on NCCN 2025 and ESMO 2024 guidelines.

▼❓ Practice Questions — Lung Cancer
Q1. A 62-year-old never-smoking East Asian woman presents with metastatic lung adenocarcinoma. NGS shows EGFR exon 19 deletion. PD-L1 TPS is 60%. What is the most appropriate first-line treatment?
Answer: Osimertinib (third-generation EGFR TKI) monotherapy
In EGFR-mutant NSCLC, targeted therapy (osimertinib) always takes priority over immunotherapy, regardless of PD-L1 expression. FLAURA trial established osimertinib as standard 1L therapy (PFS 18.9 mo; OS 38.6 mo). PD-L1 expression is NOT an indication for pembrolizumab in EGFR-mutant patients — combination with TKIs increases severe pneumonitis, and pembrolizumab monotherapy is inferior in oncogene-driven disease. FLAURA2 added chemotherapy to osimertinib (PFS 25.5 mo) for selected patients with high burden of disease.
Q2. A 55-year-old man with limited-stage SCLC achieves complete remission after 6 cycles of cisplatin/etoposide + concurrent radiotherapy. What is the recommended next step?
Answer: Prophylactic cranial irradiation (PCI) 25 Gy/10 fractions
PCI remains standard of care for LD-SCLC achieving CR/good PR after chemoradiation. Reduces brain metastasis risk from ~60% to ~33% and improves 3-year OS (20.7% vs 13.3% — Auperin meta-analysis 1999). Hippocampal-avoidant PCI reduces neurocognitive toxicity. For ED-SCLC, MRI surveillance every 3 months (rather than PCI) is now an acceptable alternative based on the Japanese trial (Takahashi NEJM 2017) — though Western practice still varies.

Gastrointestinal cancers collectively represent the most common cause of cancer death globally. Molecular profiling has transformed management, particularly in CRC (MSI/RAS/BRAF), gastric cancer (HER2/PD-L1), and cholangiocarcinoma (FGFR2/IDH1). Content follows ESMO 2024 and NCCN 2025 guidelines.

Q1. A 58-year-old man with metastatic CRC has KRAS exon 2 wild-type, NRAS wild-type, BRAF wild-type tumor. His primary tumor was in the sigmoid colon (left-sided). Which is the best first-line regimen?
Answer: FOLFOX (or FOLFIRI) + cetuximab or panitumumab (anti-EGFR therapy)
Left-sided, all-RAS/BRAF wildtype mCRC = anti-EGFR is superior to anti-VEGF. CALGB 80405 and FIRE-3 trials established this paradigm. Anti-EGFR (cetuximab/panitumumab) + FOLFOX is preferred 1L for left-sided WT tumors (OS up to 35 months with cetuximab+FOLFIRI in FIRE-3 left-sided subgroup). If right-sided: bevacizumab preferred. Q2. A 55-year-old woman with intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma progresses on gemcitabine + cisplatin + durvalumab. NGS reveals FGFR2-BICC1 fusion. What is the most appropriate next step?
Answer: Pemigatinib or futibatinib (FGFR2 inhibitor)
FGFR2 fusions/rearrangements occur in ~15-20% of intrahepatic CCA. Pemigatinib (FIGHT-202: ORR 37%) and futibatinib (FOENIX-CCA2: ORR 42%) are both FDA-approved for FGFR2 fusion-positive biliary tract cancer after prior therapy. Key toxicities: hyperphosphatemia (monitor serum phosphate; dietary phosphate restriction, sevelamer), dry eye/keratopathy (ophthalmology monitoring required), nail changes, stomatitis. FGFR2 inhibitors are highly effective in this molecularly-selected population.

4. Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

HCC is the most common primary liver cancer and the 4th leading cause of cancer-related death worldwide. It almost exclusively arises in the setting of chronic liver disease. Multidisciplinary management integrating hepatology, interventional radiology, surgery, and oncology is essential. Treatment decisions are governed by liver function (Child-Pugh/ALBI), tumor burden, and performance status — the BCLC staging system.

▼❓ Practice Questions — HCC
Q1. A 58-year-old man with HCV cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A, ECOG PS 0) is found to have a single 4 cm LR-5 lesion in segment 6 with no vascular invasion or extrahepatic spread. He has no esophageal varices on recent endoscopy. What is the MOST appropriate first step?
Answer: Surgical resection (preferred) or proceed to liver transplant evaluation (BCLC A)
BCLC A (single nodule, preserved liver function, PS 0) → curative intent. Surgical resection is preferred if adequate functional liver reserve (ICGR15 <20%, future liver remnant adequate). Liver transplant evaluation is ideal if the patient has significant portal hypertension or marginal hepatic reserve (within Milan criteria). This patient has no varices (no portal hypertension) and Child-Pugh A → resection is preferred. Ablation (RFA) is an alternative if <3 cm or surgically unfit. The LR-5 designation (arterial enhancement + washout) confirms HCC diagnosis without biopsy. Do NOT start systemic therapy for localized, resectable disease.
Q2. A 65-year-old man with HBV-associated HCC (BCLC C — portal vein thrombosis, 2 liver lesions, Child-Pugh A, PS 1) is started on atezolizumab + bevacizumab. Three weeks later he presents with hematemesis. What was the critical pre-treatment step that was missed?
Answer: Upper endoscopy to evaluate and treat esophageal varices BEFORE starting bevacizumab
Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF) inhibits VEGF-mediated vascular healing and is CONTRAINDICATED in patients with untreated esophageal varices at high risk of bleeding. In cirrhotic patients with portal hypertension (as in this case — HBV cirrhosis), variceal bleeding risk is significant. NCCN/EASL guidelines mandate upper endoscopy BEFORE starting atezo-bev. If high-risk varices found: prophylactic band ligation or non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol/carvedilol) → then start atezo-bev. If patient bleeds on bevacizumab: discontinue permanently. Consider STRIDE regimen (durvalumab + tremelimumab) as alternative when bevacizumab is contraindicated.
Q3. A 52-year-old woman with NASH cirrhosis and HCC has AFP levels of 650 ng/mL. She responds to 2 cycles of TACE with 60% tumor necrosis on MRI. She is on the liver transplant waitlist (within Milan criteria). What is the primary concern regarding her transplant candidacy?
Answer: AFP level >1000 ng/mL at time of transplant evaluation predicts poor post-transplant outcomes (HCC recurrence)
AFP trajectory and absolute level at time of transplant are critical predictors of post-transplant HCC recurrence. AFP >1000 ng/mL at listing (or persistently elevated despite treatment) is a relative contraindication to transplant in many centers (associated with 2× higher recurrence risk). AFP decline after treatment (e.g., TACE): if AFP normalizes or drops significantly → favorable. This patient's AFP of 650 is concerning but not prohibitive. Key: monitor AFP trajectory with bridging therapy. If AFP rises despite bridging → suggests aggressive biology → reconsider transplant or wait for more tumor control. Patients with AFP >1000 at transplant have 5-year survival ~40% vs ~65% with AFP <200.

Q1. A 48-year-old premenopausal woman with ER+/PR+/HER2− stage II breast cancer completes neoadjuvant chemotherapy and surgery. Residual disease of 2 cm is found in the breast. BRCA1 germline mutation is confirmed. What is the best adjuvant systemic therapy plan?
Answer: Olaparib × 1 year (per OlympiA) + endocrine therapy (tamoxifen or AI + ovarian suppression)

Q2. A 62-year-old woman with HR+/HER2− metastatic breast cancer develops bone and liver metastases while on palbociclib + letrozole (2 years). Next-generation sequencing of ctDNA reveals an ESR1 Y537S mutation. What is the preferred next-line therapy?
Answer: Elacestrant (oral SERD) monotherapy or fulvestrant-based combination
ESR1 mutations (Y537S, D538G) are the most common acquired resistance mechanism to aromatase inhibitors + CDK4/6 inhibitors, found in 20-40% of patients after CDK4/6i progression. Elacestrant (EMERALD trial, NEJM 2023) is an oral selective estrogen receptor degrader (SERD) that outperforms standard-of-care endocrine therapy in ESR1-mutant patients: PFS 3.8 vs 1.9 months (HR 0.55 in ESR1-mutant subgroup). It is FDA approved for ESR1-mutant HR+/HER2− mBC post-CDK4/6i. Alternative: capivasertib + fulvestrant if PIK3CA/AKT/PTEN pathway also altered (CAPItello-291). Fulvestrant + everolimus is less effective in ESR1-mutant disease (fulvestrant degradation of mutant ER is less effective).

⚡ High-Yield: irAE General Principles
  • Timing: Most irAEs occur within weeks-months of ICI start; endocrine irAEs can be delayed (months to years); irAEs can occur even after ICI discontinuation (immune memory)
  • Anti-CTLA-4 vs Anti-PD-1/L1: Ipilimumab causes more severe/early GI irAEs; PD-1/L1 cause more endocrine/pulmonary irAEs; combination = additive irAE risk (50% any grade, 20% grade 3-4)
  • Re-challenge: After grade 1-2 irAE resolution and steroid taper → may rechallenge (with close monitoring); grade 3-4 irAEs → generally permanently discontinue ICI

Q1. A 68-year-old man with metastatic NSCLC on pembrolizumab monotherapy presents with 2 weeks of progressive dyspnea, dry cough, and low-grade fever. CXR shows bilateral infiltrates. Answer: Grade 2-3 ICI Pneumonitis — HOLD ICI, bronchoscopy + BAL to exclude infection, start prednisone 1-2 mg/kg/day

Q2. A 55-year-old woman on nivolumab + ipilimumab for metastatic melanoma presents with headache, fatigue, and nausea. TSH is 0.05 mU/L, free T4 is 2.8 ng/dL (elevated), AM cortisol is 3 μg/dL, ACTH is 8 pg/mL (low-normal). MRI pituitary shows a 12 mm enlarged pituitary gland. What is the diagnosis and management?
Answer: Hypophysitis with secondary adrenal insufficiency and thyrotoxicosis (early phase)
Hypophysitis: enlarged pituitary on MRI + headache + secondary adrenal insufficiency (low cortisol + low-normal ACTH — pituitary fails to produce ACTH) + thyrotoxicosis (transient phase of thyroiditis coexisting or hypophysitis-related TSH surge then suppression). Key steps: (1) Start HYDROCORTISONE FIRST (100 mg IV bolus, then 100 mg/day) — BEFORE any thyroid treatment — to prevent adrenal crisis; (2) Thyrotoxicosis: beta-blocker for symptoms; monitor; typically self-limiting; (3) High-dose dexamethasone (0.1 mg/kg) for 2-4 weeks for pituitary mass reduction, then taper; (4) HOLD ICI; (5) Hormone axes: monitor recovery — most patients with ICI hypophysitis never recover (permanent hormone deficiency is common). This is predominantly an anti-CTLA-4 irAE.

▼7.2 Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

TLS results from rapid destruction of malignant cells → release of intracellular contents (nucleic acids, phosphate, potassium) → hyperuricemia, hyperphosphatemia, hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia → AKI, arrhythmia, seizures, cardiac arrest.

Cairo-Bishop Definition: Laboratory TLS = ≥2 of: Uric acid ≥8 mg/dL (or 25% increase); K ≥6 mEq/L (or 25% increase); Phosphate ≥4.5 mg/dL (or 25% increase); Calcium ≤7 mg/dL (or 25% decrease). Within 3 days before to 7 days after cytotoxic therapy.

The KEY discriminator is VOLUME STATUS. SIADH = euvolemic or mildly hypervolemic → fluid restriction. CSW = hypovolemic (orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia, poor turgor) → fluid replacement. Giving fluid restriction to CSW causes worsening hypovolemia and can precipitate cerebral vasospasm. Check clinical volume status and BNP/ANP levels. In patients with hyponatremia after neurosurgery or cranial irradiation: always think CSW vs SIADH.
▼7.8 Hyperviscosity Syndrome

Hyperviscosity syndrome occurs when abnormal proteins or cells increase blood viscosity → impaired microvascular flow → end-organ damage. Classic in Waldenström macroglobulinemia (IgM) and multiple myeloma (IgG or IgA — less common; IgM most viscous).

AspectDetails
Classic TriadBleeding (mucocutaneous: epistaxis, gingival, fundal hemorrhage); Visual changes (retinal vein engorgement → "sausage links," retinal hemorrhage, visual blurring, diplopia → fundoscopy critical); Neurological (headache, altered consciousness, stroke-like episodes, vertigo, hearing loss — "pseudo-stroke")

TreatmentEmergent plasmapheresis (plasma exchange) — removes paraprotein; rapidly reduces viscosity; indicated when symptomatic; repeat q24-48h until symptoms resolve; 1 plasma volume exchange reduces IgM by 50-60%; bridge to definitive treatment. Definitive: Rituximab-based regimens (Waldenström: R-CHOP, BR, ibrutinib + rituximab); bortezomib for IgG/IgA myeloma. Avoid: Red cell transfusions (worsen viscosity) until plasmapheresis initiated; IV fluids cautiously (haemodilution benefit but monitor volume status).

Q1. A 55-year-old man with AML undergoes induction chemotherapy with 7+3 (cytarabine + idarubicin). On day 2, labs show: K 6.8 mEq/L, phosphate 6.2 mg/dL, uric acid 11.2 mg/dL, Ca 6.4 mg/dL, creatinine 2.8 mg/dL (baseline 0.9). Urine output is 30 mL/hr. What is the diagnosis and most critical immediate intervention?
Answer: Tumor Lysis Syndrome with AKI — Emergent management with rasburicase + aggressive hydration ± dialysis
This meets laboratory TLS criteria (Cairo-Bishop): hyperuricemia 11.2 mg/dL, hyperkalemia 6.8, hyperphosphatemia 6.2, hypocalcemia 6.4 — 4/4 criteria + AKI (creatinine 3× baseline). AML induction = highest TLS risk. Management: (1) Rasburicase 0.2 mg/kg IV (rapid uric acid reduction; check G6PD first); (2) Aggressive IV NS hydration (200+ mL/hr) to target urine output 100-150 mL/hr — if oliguria persists → assess for fluid overload vs true AKI; (3) Hyperkalemia: calcium gluconate (cardiac protection), insulin-dextrose, sodium bicarbonate, kayexalate; (4) Hypocalcemia: DO NOT correct asymptomatic hypocalcemia (Ca×P product already high — precipitation will worsen); treat only if tetany/arrhythmia; (5) NEPHROLOGY CONSULTATION early — dialysis likely needed given AKI + severe electrolyte disturbances. Avoid adding K or phosphate to IV fluids.
Q2. A 67-year-old woman with metastatic breast cancer (bone mets) presents with confusion, constipation, polyuria, and nausea. Calcium is 14.8 mg/dL (corrected). She is dehydrated. PTHrP is elevated; PTH is suppressed. What is the sequence of treatment?
Answer: (1) Aggressive IV saline hydration → (2) Zoledronic acid IV → (3) Calcitonin if rapid effect needed → Treat underlying cancer
Malignant hypercalcemia (PTHrP-mediated): Ca 14.8 = severe (>14 mg/dL). Treatment sequence: Step 1: NS 200-500 mL/hour (expand volume, promote calciuresis — the most immediate intervention; loop diuretics ONLY after euvolemia restored). Step 2: Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV over 15-30 min (onset 24-72h; peak 4-7 days; most effective agent; hold if GFR <35 → use denosumab instead). Step 3: Calcitonin 4-8 IU/kg SC q6-12h (fastest onset, hours; limited to 24-48h before tachyphylaxis; useful bridge to bisphosphonate). Step 4: Treat underlying cancer (her breast cancer is driving the hypercalcemia — systemic therapy is the definitive solution). Do NOT use furosemide until well-hydrated. Denosumab 120 mg SC if renal insufficiency present (no dose adjustment needed).
Q3. A 70-year-old man with metastatic prostate cancer presents with 3 days of progressive lower extremity weakness, urinary retention, and a sensory level at T10. He can still walk with assistance. MRI confirms T9 epidural compression with 50% spinal cord compromise. What is the most important first step and the most important prognostic factor?
Answer: Dexamethasone immediately (16 mg IV bolus) — Neurological function at presentation is the most important prognostic factor
First step: Dexamethasone 16 mg IV immediately (do NOT wait for surgical or radiation consultation — start steroids NOW; reduces cord edema within hours; may prevent progression to complete paralysis). Then urgent surgical evaluation vs RT decision: Prostate cancer is generally radiosensitive → RT is appropriate in most; BUT if solitary level + ambulatory → consider Patchell study criteria (surgery + RT superior to RT alone if ambulation preservation is goal and surgical candidate). Prognostic factor: ambulatory status at start of treatment is the MOST important determinant of ambulatory outcome. He can still walk → if treated urgently, ~80-90% chance of maintaining ambulation. Complete paraplegia >48h → <5-10% chance of recovery. Radioresistant tumors (RCC, melanoma, sarcoma) → SBRT preferred over EBRT.
