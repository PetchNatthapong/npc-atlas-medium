# Long QT Syndrome: Comprehensive Review for Cardiologists

**Topic:** Long QT Syndrome — Types, Diagnostic Criteria & Clinical Approach
**Level:** Expert (Cardiologist)
**Date:** April 2026

---

## 📋 Abstract

Long QT syndrome (LQTS) is a cardiac channelopathy characterized by delayed ventricular repolarization, predisposing to torsades de pointes (TdP), syncope, and sudden cardiac death (SCD). Prevalence is approximately 1:2,000–7,000. This review covers all identified genetic subtypes (LQT1–LQT17+), diagnostic criteria including the Schwartz score, exercise and pharmacological provocative testing, genetic testing strategies, and risk stratification.

---

## 1. Definition & Epidemiology

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Prevalence** | ~1:2,000–7,000 |
| **Sex** | Female > Male |
| **Age of symptom onset** | Majority before age 40; peak in adolescence |
| **Annual SCD (US)** | ~3,500 deaths/year |
| **Untreated mortality** | >50% within 15 years after cardiac arrest |
| **Treated mortality** | <1% over 20 years |

### Normal QTc Values

| Population | Normal | Borderline | Prolonged |
|------------|--------|------------|-----------|
| Adult males | <430 ms | 430–450 ms | >450 ms |
| Adult females | <450 ms | 450–470 ms | >470 ms |
| Children (1–15 yr) | <440 ms | 440–460 ms | >460 ms |

> **Note:** Bazett correction (QT/√RR) overcorrects at high HR, undercorrects at low HR. Fridericia (QT/∛RR) is preferred at HR extremes.

## 2. Classification: Congenital vs Acquired

| Feature | Congenital (cLQTS) | Acquired (aLQTS) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Etiology | Genetic variants in ion channels | Drugs, electrolytes, bradycardia, hypothyroidism |
| Inheritance | Autosomal dominant (most) / recessive (JLN) | None |
| Reversibility | Lifelong | Often reversible |
| TdP mechanism | Early afterdepolarizations (EADs) | Same (EADs) |
| Key examples | Romano-Ward, Jervell & Lange-Nielsen | Drug-induced (antiarrhythmics, antibiotics, antipsychotics) |

### Major Clinical Syndromes

| Syndrome | Inheritance | Features | LQT Types |
|----------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Romano-Ward** | AD | Isolated cardiac (no deafness) | LQT1-6, LQT9-17 |
| **Jervell & Lange-Nielsen (JLN)** | AR | LQTS + congenital sensorineural deafness | LQT1 (KCNQ1 biallelic), LQT5 (KCNE1 biallelic) |
| **Andersen-Tawil (ATS)** | AD | LQTS + periodic paralysis + dysmorphic features | LQT7 (KCNJ2) |
| **Timothy syndrome (TS)** | AD/Sporadic | LQTS + syndactyly + ASD + cardiac defects | LQT8 (CACNA1C) |

## 3. Genotype-Phenotype Correlation (LQT1–LQT17)

### 3.1 The Major Three (≥90% of genetically confirmed cases)

| Type | Gene | Chromosome | Ion Current | Mechanism | Prevalence | Typical Triggers |
|------|------|------------|-------------|-----------|------------|------------------|
| **LQT1** | KCNQ1 | 11p15.5 | IKs (slow delayed rectifier K⁺) | Loss of function | 30–35% | Exercise (esp. swimming), emotional stress, sympathetic activation |
| **LQT2** | KCNH2 (hERG) | 7q36 | IKr (rapid delayed rectifier K⁺) | Loss of function | 25–30% | Auditory stimuli (alarm clock, phone), emotional stress, postpartum |
| **LQT3** | SCN5A | 3p22–24 | INa (late Na⁺) | Gain of function (persistent late INa) | 5–10% | Rest, sleep, bradycardia |

### 3.2 Minor/Rare Subtypes

| Type | Gene | Ion Current / Protein | Key Features | Prevalence |
|------|------|-----------------------|-------------|------------|
| LQT4 | ANK2 (Ankyrin-B) | Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase, InsP3 targeting | "Ankyrin-B syndrome"; sinus node dysfunction, AF | <1% |
| LQT5 | KCNE1 (minK) | IKs β-subunit | Heterozygous: mild LQTS; Biallelic: JLN with deafness | <1% |
| LQT6 | KCNE2 (MiRP1) | IKr β-subunit | Drug-sensitive QT prolongation | Very rare |
| **LQT7** | **KCNJ2** (Kir2.1) | IK1 (inward rectifier) | **Andersen-Tawil syndrome**: periodic paralysis, facial dysmorphism, ventricular ectopy, prominent U waves | ~1% |
| **LQT8** | **CACNA1C** (Cav1.2) | ICa-L (L-type Ca²⁺) | **Timothy syndrome**: syndactyly, autism, cardiac malformations, very long QT (>500ms), high mortality | Very rare |
| LQT9 | CAV3 (Caveolin-3) | Na⁺ channel regulation | Associated with SIDS; exercise triggers | Very rare |
| LQT10 | SCN4B (NaVβ4) | INa β-subunit | Very rare; gain of late INa | Very rare |
| LQT11 | AKAP9 (Yotiao) | IKs regulatory protein | K⁺ channel targeting; rare | Very rare |
| LQT12 | SNTA1 (α1-syntrophin) | Na⁺ channel regulation | SCN5A membrane targeting; rare | Very rare |
| LQT13 | KCNJ5 (Kir3.4) | IK(ACh) | Associated with AF | Very rare |
| LQT14 | CALM1 (Calmodulin) | Ca²⁺ sensing | Severe LQTS, onset in infancy, seizures overlap | Very rare |
| LQT15 | CALM2 (Calmodulin) | Ca²⁺ sensing | Similar to CALM1; infantile onset | Very rare |
| LQT16 | CALM3 (Calmodulin) | Ca²⁺ sensing | Similar to CALM1/2 | Very rare |
| LQT17 | TRDN (Triadin) | Ca²⁺ release complex | "Triadin knockout syndrome"; autosomal recessive, pediatric cardiac arrest | Very rare |

### 3.3 Gene-Specific ECG Patterns

| Genotype | T-wave Morphology | QT Response to Exercise |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **LQT1** | Broad-based, smooth T waves; normal ST segment | Poor QT shortening (flat QT-HR relationship) |
| **LQT2** | Low-amplitude, notched/bifid T waves | Normal QT shortening, but prominent recovery QTc prolongation |
| **LQT3** | Late-onset T waves; long ST segment with sharp T wave | Excessive QT shortening with exercise (paradoxical risk at rest) |
| **LQT7** | Prominent U waves, prolonged QU interval | Variable |

## 4. Diagnostic Criteria

### 4.1 Schwartz Diagnostic Score (2011/Updated)

| Criterion | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| **QTc** ≥480 ms | 3 |
| QTc 460–479 ms | 2 |
| QTc 450–459 ms (male) | 1 |
| **Torsades de pointes** | 2 |
| **T-wave alternans** | 1 |
| **Notched T waves** in 3 leads | 1 |
| **Low heart rate** for age (children) | 0.5 |
| **Syncope** (with stress) | 2 |
| Syncope (without stress) | 1 |
| **Congenital deafness** | 0.5 |
| **Family history** of LQTS | 1 |
| Family history of SCD <30 yr | 0.5 |

**Interpretation:**

| Score | Probability |
|-------|-------------|
| ≤1 | Low probability |
| 1.5–3 | Intermediate |
| ≥3.5 | High probability → diagnose LQTS |

### 4.2 QTc Cutoffs for Diagnosis (2023 HRS/ACC/AHA Guidelines)

| QTc (ms) | Classification |
|----------|---------------|
| ≥500 | **Definite LQTS** (even without symptoms/family history) |
| 480–499 | **High probability** (especially if symptoms present) |
| 460–479 | **Borderline** — provocative testing recommended |
| <460 | **Normal range** — does NOT exclude LQTS (concealed LQTS) |

> **Key point:** ~25–35% of genotype-positive LQTS patients have QTc <460 ms at rest ("concealed LQTS"). Provocative testing is essential.

### 4.3 Exercise Stress Testing

| Parameter | Diagnostic Value |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Recovery-phase QTc** (at 1–4 min post-exercise) | Most useful single metric; QTc >480 ms in recovery is highly suggestive of LQTS |
| **LQT1**: failure to shorten QT during exercise | Flat QT-HR slope (IKs cannot upregulate) |
| **LQT2**: exaggerated QTc prolongation in recovery | IKr cannot compensate for increased HR |
| **LQT3**: excessive QT shortening during exercise | Paradoxically "normal" response; risk at rest |

### 4.4 Epinephrine (Adrenaline) Challenge Test

**Protocol:** Infuse epinephrine 0.025–0.1 μg/kg/min (or bolus protocol) while monitoring QTc.

| Response | Suggests | Mechanism |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| **Paradoxical QTc prolongation ≥30 ms** during low-dose epinephrine | **LQT1** | IKs cannot respond to β-adrenergic stimulation → unmasked prolongation |
| Transient prolongation then normalization | **LQT2** | IKr suppression by PKA phosphorylation is transient |
| Minimal QTc change | **LQT3** | INa gain-of-function not β-adrenergically mediated |
| Normal (QTc shortens) | Non-LQTS | Normal IKs upregulation |

> **Safety:** Requires continuous ECG monitoring, IV access, and resuscitation equipment. Not recommended in patients with known severe LQTS or recent cardiac arrest.

### 4.5 Genetic Testing

**Indications (HRS/ACC/AHA 2023):**

1. Any patient with confirmed or suspected LQTS (Schwartz ≥2)
2. Family members of genotype-positive index case (cascade testing)
3. Unexplained cardiac arrest / SCD in young person
4. Unexplained syncope with borderline QTc

**Recommended Gene Panel (Tier 1 — Definitive Evidence):**

| Gene | LQT Type | Evidence Level |
|------|----------|---------------|
| KCNQ1 | LQT1 | Definitive |
| KCNH2 | LQT2 | Definitive |
| SCN5A | LQT3 | Definitive |
| KCNE1 | LQT5/JLN | Definitive |
| KCNE2 | LQT6 | Definitive |
| KCNJ2 | LQT7/ATS | Definitive |
| CACNA1C | LQT8/TS | Definitive |

**Variant Classification (ACMG/AMP):**
- **Pathogenic / Likely Pathogenic** → clinical action
- **VUS (Variant of Uncertain Significance)** → do NOT use for clinical decisions; reclassify periodically
- **Likely Benign / Benign** → no action

> **Caveat (Adler et al., Circulation 2020):** Many genes previously associated with LQTS lack robust evidence. Only KCNQ1, KCNH2, SCN5A, KCNE1, KCNE2, KCNJ2, and CACNA1C have definitive evidence. Others should be interpreted cautiously.

## 5. Risk Stratification

### 5.1 1-2-3-LQTS-Risk Model (Mazzanti et al., Europace 2022)

Validated predictors of arrhythmic events:

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **1. QTc duration** | Every 10 ms increase → ~15% increased risk; QTc ≥500 ms = high risk |
| **2. History of syncope/cardiac arrest** | Strongest predictor of future events |
| **3. Genotype** | LQT2 ≥ LQT3 > LQT1 for arrhythmic risk at comparable QTc |

### 5.2 Additional Risk Factors

| Factor | Risk Modifier |
|--------|---------------|
| Female sex (especially post-pubertal) | Higher risk in LQT2 |
| Male sex (pre-pubertal) | Higher risk in LQT1 |
| Postpartum period | Particularly high risk in LQT2 |
| QTc ≥500 ms | High risk regardless of genotype |
| Multiple mutations (compound heterozygous) | More severe phenotype |
| Family history of SCD | Independent risk factor |
| Non-compliance with beta-blockers | Major modifiable risk |

### 5.3 Risk Categories

| Risk Category | Features | Recommended Management |
|---------------|----------|----------------------|
| **Low** | QTc <480, no syncope, LQT1 | Beta-blocker alone |
| **Intermediate** | QTc 480–500, or syncope on BB | BB + consider LCSD |
| **High** | QTc ≥500, cardiac arrest, recurrent syncope on BB | BB + ICD ± LCSD |

## 6. Management Summary

### 6.1 Beta-Blockers

| Drug | Preference | Dosing | Notes |
|------|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Nadolol** | 1st line (preferred) | 1–2.5 mg/kg/day | Long half-life; most evidence in LQT1 |
| **Propranolol** | 1st line (preferred) | 2–4 mg/kg/day | Shorter half-life; multiple daily doses |
| Atenolol | Alternative | 1–2 mg/kg/day | Once daily |
| Metoprolol | **NOT preferred** | — | Higher recurrence rate (Chockalingam et al., JACC 2012) |

> **Critical:** Metoprolol is associated with significantly higher event rates in LQT1 and LQT2 compared to nadolol/propranolol. Avoid as first-line.

### 6.2 Genotype-Specific Considerations

| Genotype | Specific Therapy |
|----------|-----------------|
| **LQT1** | Beta-blockers excellent; avoid strenuous exercise (esp. swimming) |
| **LQT2** | Beta-blockers + potassium supplementation; avoid sudden loud noises |
| **LQT3** | Mexiletine or ranolazine (late INa blockers); ICD more often needed |
| **LQT7 (ATS)** | Beta-blockers for QT; acetazolamide for periodic paralysis |
| **LQT8 (TS)** | Verapamil (ICa-L blocker); high ICD rate |

### 6.3 ICD Indications (Class I)

- Survivors of cardiac arrest
- Recurrent syncope despite maximal beta-blocker therapy
- QTc ≥550 ms with strong family history of SCD

### 6.4 Left Cardiac Sympathetic Denervation (LCSD)

- Indicated for: beta-blocker refractory symptoms, ICD storm, beta-blocker intolerance
- Removes lower half of stellate ganglion + T2–T4 ganglia
- Reduces arrhythmic events by ~90%

## 7. Take-Home Points

1. **LQTS is the most common inherited cardiac channelopathy** — think of it in any young person with unexplained syncope, seizures, or family history of SCD.
2. **Schwartz score ≥3.5 = diagnose LQTS.** But remember concealed LQTS (normal resting QTc) — use provocative testing.
3. **LQT1, LQT2, LQT3 = 90%+ of cases.** Know their trigger patterns: exercise (LQT1), auditory/emotional (LQT2), rest/sleep (LQT3).
4. **Not all beta-blockers are equal.** Nadolol and propranolol are preferred; avoid metoprolol.
5. **QTc ≥500 ms = high risk** regardless of symptoms. Every 10 ms adds ~15% arrhythmic risk.
6. **Genetic testing changes management.** Cascade testing saves lives. But only ~70% of clinically diagnosed LQTS has an identified genetic cause.
7. **Recovery QTc during exercise testing** is the single most useful ECG metric for concealed LQTS.
8. **Epinephrine challenge** is gene-specific diagnostic — paradoxical prolongation is pathognomonic for LQT1.
