# Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) Guidelines 2025: Rapid Evidence Review

## Abstract

The American Heart Association (AHA) released its 2025 Guidelines for Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) and Emergency Cardiovascular Care (ECC) on October 22, 2025 — the first comprehensive revision since 2020. Key updates span the entire chain of survival from basic life support (BLS) to advanced cardiovascular life support (ACLS), post-cardiac arrest care, systems of care, and ethics. Major changes include elimination of the two-finger infant compression technique, expanded emphasis on trained lay rescuer ventilations, a new opioid overdose algorithm, updated targeted temperature management (≥36 hours), and clearer guidance on defibrillation in women.

## 1. Overview — What's New in 2025

| Topic | 2020 → 2025 Change |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Chain of Survival** | Unified for all ages and settings |
| **Infant CPR** | Two-finger technique **ELIMINATED**; two-thumb encircling hands now preferred |
| **Lay rescuer CPR** | Trained rescuers: 30:2 compressions:breaths; hands-only still acceptable |
| **FBAO (choking)** | Updated to **5 back blows + 5 abdominal thrusts** (was "back blows OR abdominal thrusts") |
| **Women & defibrillation** | New guidance to prevent delays in women |
| **Opioid overdose** | **New algorithm** with naloxone recommendations |
| **Head-up CPR** | **Not recommended** except in clinical trials |
| **Dual sequential defibrillation** | Not established; not routine ACLS |
| **ECPR** | Selective use with formal patient selection criteria |
| **TTM duration** | Extended to **≥36 hours** (was 24 hours in 2020) |
| **Post-ROSC TTM** | Prevent fever; maintain **normothermia** (shifted from routine hypothermia) |
| **Neuroprognostication** | Multimodal approach; **≥72 hours** after ROSC/normothermia |

> **Validity:** 2025 Guidelines effective through 2030

## 2. Unified Chain of Survival

All cardiac arrests (all ages, all settings) now follow a single, simplified unified chain of survival model:

1. **Recognition & activation** —识别并激活应急反应系统
2. **Early high-quality CPR** — 早期高质量心肺复苏
3. **Rapid defibrillation** — 快速除颤
4. **Effective advanced care** — 有效的高级护理
5. **Post-cardiac arrest care** — 心脏骤停后护理
6. **Recovery & survivorship** — 康复与生存

**Key emphasis:**
- Health equity and addressing CPR disparities
- Culturally tailored training for specific racial/ethnic groups
- Addressing barriers to CPR for women (fear of causing injury, inappropriate touch)
- Expanding dispatcher-guided CPR instructions
- Public access to naloxone and AEDs

## 3. Adult BLS — Key Updates

### 3.1 Chest Compression Mechanics (Unchanged)

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Rate** | 100–120 compressions/min |
| **Depth** | ≥2 inches (≈5 cm) for adults |
| **Recoil** | Allow full chest recoil |
| **Interruptions** | Minimize to <10 seconds |
| **Hand position** | Lower half of sternum |
| **Surface** | Firm surface preferred |

> Real-time audiovisual feedback devices are now emphasized to ensure consistent high-quality CPR.

### 3.2 Ventilation — Key Change for Trained Lay Rescuers

| Rescuer Type | Recommendation |
|--------------|----------------|
| **Untrained / unwilling** | Hands-only CPR (acceptable) |
| **Trained lay rescuers** | **30:2 compressions:breaths** (when willing and able) |
| **Healthcare professionals** | 30:2 compressions:breaths |

> 💡 **Key change:** Previously, hands-only was preferred for all lay rescuers. Now, *trained* lay rescuers are specifically advised to provide ventilations.

### 3.3 Dispatcher-Guided CPR

| Patient | Dispatcher Instruction |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Adult** | Hands-only CPR (not conventional CPR) |
| **Child/Infant** | Conventional CPR with rescue breaths (30:2) |

### 3.4 Opioid Overdose — New Algorithm

The 2025 guidelines introduce a dedicated algorithm for suspected opioid overdose:

**Signs of opioid-associated emergency:**
- Unresponsive
- Respiratory depression or apnea
- Pinpoint pupils (miosis)

**Bystander/Public Response:**
1. Activate emergency response (call 1669/911)
2. Administer **naloxone** — if no response in 2–5 min, repeat dose
3. Provide CPR if trained
4. Place in recovery position

**Naloxone recommendations:**
- IM/SQ preferred by lay rescuers; auto-injector or nasal spray also acceptable
- No contraindications in suspected opioid overdose
- Community naloxone distribution programs recommended

### 3.5 Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO) — Updated

| Situation | 2020 | 2025 Update |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **Conscious adult/child with FBAO** | 5 back blows OR 5 abdominal thrusts | **5 back blows + 5 abdominal thrusts** (alternating) until cleared |
| **Unconscious FBAO** | CPR + finger sweep | CPR + jaw thrust; remove visible objects only |

## 4. Infant CPR — Major Change

### Two-Finger Technique ELIMINATED

The two-finger technique for infant chest compressions has been **removed** from the 2025 guidelines.

| Technique | 2020 | 2025 |
|----------|-------|------|
| **Two-finger** | Acceptable for single rescuers | **ELIMINATED** |
| **Two-thumb encircling hands** | Preferred for 2-rescuer | **Preferred for ALL rescuers** |
| **One-hand (heel of hand)** | Acceptable | Acceptable only if two-thumb not feasible |

**Rationale:** Two-thumb encircling hands produces higher coronary perfusion pressure and systolic BP compared to two-finger technique.

**Infant CPR summary:**
| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| Compression:breath ratio | **15:2** (single or two rescuers) |
| Compression depth | Approximately 1.5 inches (4 cm) |
| Rate | 100–120/min |
| Technique | Two-thumb encircling hands |
| Activation | 2 minutes CPR → then call emergency (if alone) |

## 5. Women and Defibrillation — New Guidance

The 2025 guidelines specifically address barriers to CPR and defibrillation in women:

### Barriers Identified
- Uncertainty about removing clothing/bras for defibrillation
- Misconceptions about causing injury
- Concerns about inappropriate touch
- Fear of harming the patient
- Delayed recognition of cardiac arrest in women

### Recommendations
- Remove clothing and undergarments (including bras) quickly for pad placement
- Defibrillation pads should be placed directly on skin
- Standard anterolateral pad placement applies — no special modification needed for women
- All rescuers should be trained to defibrillate women without hesitation
- Public education campaigns specifically targeting CPR for women

## 6. Adult ACLS — Key Updates

### 6.1 Defibrillation

| Parameter | 2020 | 2025 Update |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **First shock energy (biphasic)** | 120–200 J | **≥200 J** preferred for first shock |
| **AF/Flutter cardioversion** | 100–200 J | **≥200 J** first shock |
| **Dual sequential defibrillation (DSD)** | Not addressed | **Not recommended** as routine ACLS; usefulness not established; further study needed |
| **Head-up CPR** | Not recommended | **Not recommended** except within clinical trials |

### 6.2 Vasopressors & Epinephrine

| Drug | 2025 Recommendation |
|------|-------------------|
| **Epinephrine** | Standard vasopressor for adult cardiac arrest; continue every 3–5 minutes |
| **Vasopressin** | No advantage over epinephrine (alone or in combination) |
| **Timing (shockable)** | Defibrillation FIRST; epinephrine after initial shocks fail |
| **Timing (non-shockable)** | Consider initial epinephrine early, especially in children |

> ⚠️ No superiority of any vasopressor combination has been established.

### 6.3 Advanced Airway

| Setting | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Initial airway** | Bag-mask ventilation with O₂ |
| **Advanced airway** | Endotracheal tube or supraglottic airway |
| **ETT confirmation** | Waveform capnography (PETCO₂ ≥10 mmHg after ROSC) |

### 6.4 Electrical therapies — Summary

| Therapy | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Defibrillation (VF/pVT)** | Biphasic ≥200 J; monophasic 360 J |
| **Synchronized cardioversion (SVT/AF)** | ≥200 J first shock |
| **Transcutaneous pacing** | For symptomatic bradycardia or pacing-dependent patients |
| **DSD** | Not routine; not established |

## 7. Post-Cardiac Arrest Care

### 7.1 Targeted Temperature Management (TTM) — Major Update

| Parameter | 2020 | 2025 Update |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **Target temperature** | 32–36°C | **Prevent fever; maintain normothermia** |
| **Duration** | 24 hours | **≥36 hours** |
| **Method** | Any TTM method | Continuous temperature monitoring; active control required |

> 💡 **Paradigm shift:** From routine induction of hypothermia (32–36°C) → **actively prevent fever and maintain sustained controlled normothermia**

### 7.2 Post-ROSC Hemodynamics

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| **MAP** | **≥65 mmHg** |
| **O₂ saturation** | 94–98% (avoid hyperoxia) |
| **PaCO₂** | Normocapnia (35–45 mmHg) |
| **Hemodynamic support** | Vasopressors + temporary mechanical circulatory support if refractory shock |

### 7.3 Early Evaluation

| Test | Indication |
|------|------------|
| **12-lead ECG** | All post-ROSC patients — identify STEMI |
| **Coronary angiography** | Emergency: STEMI, shock, or instability with suspected cardiac cause |
| **Coronary angiography (non-STEMI)** | NOT routinely recommended for comatose non-STEMI patients |
| **CT brain** | Suspected neurological cause |
| **CT chest** | Suspected pulmonary cause |
| **Bedside ultrasound** | Recommended to investigate etiology and complications |

### 7.4 Neuroprognostication

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Timing** | **≥72 hours** after ROSC and normothermia (multimodal) |
| **Method** | Multimodal approach (clinical, EEG, imaging, biomarkers) |
| **EEG** | Continuous monitoring when available; early EEG for seizure detection |
| **Pediatric** | Continuous EEG strongly recommended when available |
| **Seizures** | High suspicion for myoclonus; treat aggressively |

| **Cannulation** | Clinicians performing peripheral ECPR cannulation should be experienced in percutaneous techniques |

## 9. Opioid Overdose — Public Access Algorithm

**New standalone algorithm** for suspected opioid overdose:

### Signs of Opioid Overdose
- Unresponsive to stimulation
- Respiratory depression or apnea
- Pinpoint pupils (miosis)
- Cyanosis

### Public/Bystander Response
1. **Activate emergency response** (call 1669/911/thai emergency)
2. **Administer naloxone** — IM/SQ preferred by lay rescuers
3. **Start CPR** if trained and no pulse/breathing
4. **Recovery position** if breathing normally but unresponsive
5. **Repeat naloxone** if no response in 2–5 minutes

### Naloxone Dosing
| Formulation | Dose |
|-------------|------|
| IM/SQ (0.4 mg/mL) | 0.4–1 mg; repeat if needed |
| Auto-injector | Per device instructions |
| Nasal spray | Per device instructions |

> 💡 **Community naloxone distribution programs** are recommended to increase public access and reduce opioid-related mortality.

## 11. Take-Home Points

> 1. **BLS fundamentals unchanged** — 100–120/min compressions, depth ≥2 inches, minimize interruptions
> 2. **Trained lay rescuers: 30:2** — not hands-only if trained and willing
> 3. **Infant CPR: two-thumb only** — two-finger technique eliminated
> 4. **FBAO: 5+5** — alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts
> 5. **Women: defibrillate without hesitation** — remove clothing quickly, no special pad modification needed
> 6. **New opioid algorithm** — naloxone is critical; give immediately; community distribution programs recommended
> 7. **Head-up CPR & DSD: NOT recommended** as routine ACLS
> 8. **TTM: ≥36 hours of normothermia** — prevent fever, don't routinely induce hypothermia
> 9. **Post-ROSC MAP ≥65 mmHg** — hemodynamic optimization alongside TTM
> 10. **Neuroprognostication: multimodal at ≥72 hours** — no single test is definitive
