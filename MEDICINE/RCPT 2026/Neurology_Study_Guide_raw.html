1. Cerebrovascular Disease

Cerebrovascular disease encompasses acute ischemic stroke (AIS), intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH), cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVT), and subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH). This section integrates the 2026 AHA/ASA Guidelines and landmark trial evidence through 2025.

1.1 Acute Ischemic Stroke (AIS)

Acute ischemic stroke (AIS) accounts for approximately 85% of all strokes. This section covers diagnosis, imaging, hyperacute reperfusion therapy, acute medical management, complications, secondary prevention, and special populations. Content is written at neurologist level, incorporating the 2026 AHA/ASA Guidelines for Early Management of Acute Ischemic Stroke.

1.1 Epidemiology and Global Burden

Stroke is the second leading cause of death and the third leading cause of combined death and disability worldwide. In Thailand, stroke is the second most common cause of death with an age-standardized incidence of approximately 150-200/100,000 person-years. Approximately 85% are ischemic; 10-15% are ICH; 3-5% are SAH.

1.2.2 ESUS — Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source

ESUS (Hart RG et al., 2014) is defined as non-lacunar infarct without proximal arterial stenosis (>50%) or major cardioembolic source, detected after standard workup. It represents ~25% of ischemic strokes and a heterogeneous entity.

  • Rivaroxaban vs aspirin — no benefit, more bleeding (Hart 2018, NEJM).

  • Dabigatran vs aspirin — no benefit (Diener 2019, NEJM).

  • Apixaban vs aspirin in ESUS with atrial cardiopathy markers (LA diameter >4.6 cm, NT-proBNP >250 pg/mL, PTFV1 >5000 mcV-ms) — no significant benefit in overall cohort. Subgroup with atrial cardiopathy trended toward benefit but underpowered.

  • Antiplatelet therapy remains standard. Implantable loop recorder (ILR) for 3+ years to detect occult AF — detection rate 30% at 3 years (CRYSTAL-AF). When AF found, start anticoagulation.

CLINICAL PEARL

Top-of-basilar syndrome: Sudden onset of altered consciousness, bilateral visual disturbance (hemianopia, cortical blindness), and behavioral changes (agitation, confusion, memory disturbance). Pupillary abnormalities (III nerve palsy, skew deviation) and vertical gaze palsy are hallmarks. This is a catastrophic emergency — call for thrombectomy immediately. Do NOT attribute altered consciousness to other causes without ruling out basilar occlusion.

1.4 Acute Neuroimaging — Neurologist Interpretation

1.4.1 Non-Contrast CT (NCCT) — Key Findings

  • Hyperdensity of vessel = thrombus/clot. MCA rod sign (proximal M1) vs. MCA dot sign (M2/M3). Dense basilar = basilar artery occlusion. Sensitivity ~40% but highly specific.

  • Loss of insular ribbon (insular cortex), obscuration of lentiform nucleus, loss of grey-white differentiation. Appear within 3-6 hours.

  • 10-point scoring of MCA territory. Deduct 1 point per early ischemic change in: M1-M6 (MCA cortical zones), insula (I), lentiform nucleus (L), internal capsule (IC), caudate (C). Score 0=entire territory; 10=normal. Score >=6 predicts acceptable EVT outcome in 0-6h window. Score 3-5: selective EVT benefit (see Section 1.6).

  • Single-phase CTA identifies LVO with >95% sensitivity. Multiphase CTA (mCTA) assesses collateral circulation: ESCAPE collateral scoring (0=absent, 3=prominent). Better collaterals = larger ischemic penumbra = greater EVT benefit.

1.4.2 CT Perfusion (CTP) — Interpretation for Stroke Physicians

Parameter Definition Infarct Core Penumbra Benign Oligemia
CBF (Cerebral Blood Flow) Volume of blood/unit time/tissue mass <30% of contralateral 30-60% >60%
CBV (Cerebral Blood Volume) Total blood volume in tissue Reduced (<38% contralateral) Normal or slightly reduced Normal
MTT (Mean Transit Time) Average capillary transit time Markedly prolonged Prolonged Mildly prolonged
Tmax Time to maximum of residue function >10 sec = core; >6 sec = penumbra Tmax 6-10 sec Tmax <6 sec

Mismatch ratio = Penumbra volume / Infarct core volume. Ratio >=1.8 (EXTEND criteria) or >=1.2 with absolute difference >=15 mL supports reperfusion benefit. Automated software (RAPID, VIZ.AI) generates mismatch maps for clinical decision-making.

CLINICAL PEARL

Pitfalls in CT Perfusion: (1) Low cardiac output states cause global CBF reduction — do not misinterpret as bilateral penumbra. (2) Motion artifact invalidates CTP maps. (3) Small posterior fossa structures are poorly visualized. (4) CTP overestimates core in early stroke — DWI-MRI is the gold standard for core assessment. Use CTP as guide, not absolute truth.

1.5 Intravenous Thrombolysis — AHA 2026 Updated

1.5.1 Alteplase (rtPA) — Standard Protocol

Alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (maximum 90 mg): 10% given as IV bolus over 1 minute, remaining 90% infused over 60 minutes. Eligible window: 0-4.5 hours from symptom onset/last known well (LKW).

ALTEPLASE — AHA 2026 ELIGIBILITY (UPDATED)
Onset-to-treatment: within 4.5 hours (Class I, Level A)
BP MUST be <185/110 before bolus; use IV labetalol 10-20 mg or nicardipine infusion
Glucose must be 50-400 mg/dL; correct hypoglycemia first
REMOVED contraindications (AHA 2026 updates):
— Age >80 no longer excludes from 3-4.5h window (Class IIa)
— Prior stroke + diabetes no longer excluded from 3-4.5h window (Class IIa)
— Anticoagulant use: tPA if INR<1.7, PTT normal, DOAC last dose >48h OR dabigatran level <50 ng/mL
— Minor stroke / isolated aphasia / cortical signs: tPA is REASONABLE even if 'mild' (Class IIa, AHA 2026)
Absolute contraindications: Active intracranial hemorrhage; recent intracranial/spinal surgery <3 months; head trauma <3 months; intracranial neoplasm in surgical path; aortic arch dissection

1.5.2 Tenecteplase (TNK) — AHA 2026 Preferred Agent Pre-EVT

Tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg (maximum 25 mg) given as a SINGLE IV bolus. FDA approved March 2025 for acute ischemic stroke.

CLINICAL PEARL

AHA 2026 KEY CHANGE: Tenecteplase (0.25 mg/kg, single bolus, max 25 mg) is now preferred over alteplase specifically in patients who are candidates for endovascular therapy — the single bolus allows faster transfer to the angio suite. For patients NOT receiving EVT, either agent is acceptable. NEVER use tenecteplase 0.4 mg/kg (higher dose shown inferior and unsafe).

1.6 Mechanical Thrombectomy (EVT) — AHA 2026 Updated

1.6.1 Standard Window (0-6 hours)

Class I, Level A recommendation. Five landmark trials (MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, EXTEND-IA, SWIFT PRIME, REVASCAT) established EVT benefit. Updated pooled data show NNT=2.6 for functional independence — among the most effective acute interventions in medicine.

EVT ELIGIBILITY — 0-6 HOURS (AHA 2026 Class I)
Occlusion site: ICA, MCA-M1 (Class I); MCA-M2 (Class IIa); ACA, PCA, basilar, vertebral (Class IIb)
Pre-stroke mRS 0-1 (Class I); mRS 2 may be considered (Class IIb)
NIHSS >= 6 for anterior circulation LVO
ASPECTS >= 6 on NCCT or DWI-MRI (Class I)
ASPECTS 3-5 on NCCT: EVT reasonable (Class IIa, NEW 2026) — SELECT-2, ANGEL-ASPECT, TENSION trials
IV thrombolysis eligible patients should receive tPA; do NOT delay EVT for tPA infusion
Groin puncture target: door-to-puncture <90 min; onset-to-puncture <180 min

1.6.3 Low ASPECTS (3-5) — New 2026 Evidence

Three landmark trials published 2022-2023 established EVT benefit in large core infarction (low ASPECTS):

  • EVT vs medical management in LVO with ASPECTS 3-5 or core >50 mL. EVT superior: mRS 0-3: 20% vs 7% (NNH reasonable); reduced functional dependence.

  • Chinese multicenter trial; ASPECTS 3-5 or core 70-100 mL; EVT improved outcome (mRS distribution, OR 1.37).

  • European trial; core volume 50-150 mL; EVT significantly improved outcome (mRS 0-3: 30% vs 9%, OR 4.3).

AHA 2026: EVT is now REASONABLE (Class IIa) for ASPECTS 3-5 in selected patients, particularly those with NIHSS >=6, treatable occlusion, and without contraindications. Shared decision-making with patient/family is essential due to residual disability.

1.6.5 Distal and Medium Vessel Occlusions (DMVO)

M2, M3, ACA, PCA distal occlusions account for ~30% of LVOs but historically excluded from trials. Emerging evidence:

  • EVT vs no EVT for M2 occlusions — signal toward benefit, particularly for M2 proximal.

  • Medium vessel occlusion EVT trial; results pending full publication.

  • EVT for M2 occlusions is REASONABLE. M3 and distal: Class IIb with appropriate patient selection and experienced centers.

1.6.6 Tandem Occlusion (Extracranial + Intracranial)

  • Cervical ICA occlusion + intracranial M1 occlusion: occurs in ~10-15% of LVO patients.

  • Retrograde vs antegrade approach; carotid stenting at time of EVT (TITAN registry) or staged approach. Emergent carotid stenting + EVT appears beneficial; antiplatelet/anticoagulation management post-stenting is complex.

  • Emergent extracranial ICA angioplasty/stenting during EVT may be considered. Antiplatelet loading (aspirin + clopidogrel or GPI) peri-procedurally.

1.7.2 Glycemic and Temperature Management

  • Blood glucose >180 mg/dL is independently associated with worse outcomes (larger final infarct, higher mortality). Target 140-180 mg/dL with insulin. Avoid hypoglycemia (<80 mg/dL) equally rigorously — hypoglycemia mimics and worsens stroke.

  • Treat fever (temp >37.5 C) aggressively with acetaminophen. Fever worsens excitotoxicity, edema, and BBB disruption. Therapeutic hypothermia is NOT currently recommended for ischemic stroke (clinical trials failed to show benefit).

  • Supplemental O2 only if SpO2 <94%. Routine O2 supplementation in normoxic patients has no benefit (SIESTA trial). Screen for sleep apnea — obstructive sleep apnea is highly prevalent post-stroke and treatable.

1.7.3 Dysphagia Screening and Nutritional Support

  • Mandatory BEFORE oral intake of any food/water/medication. Simple water swallow test (50 mL) or validated tool (GUSS, TOR-BSST). If fails, NPO and formal speech pathology assessment within 24h.

  • Dysphagia screening within 24h of admission for all acute stroke patients is Class I recommendation. Associated with reduced pneumonia and shorter LOS.

  • Early enteral feeding if unable to maintain PO nutrition. Avoid total parenteral nutrition unless enteral route unavailable >7 days. FOOD trial: PEG tube feeds not superior to NGT at 2-3 weeks.

1.7.4 Antiplatelet and Anticoagulation in Acute Phase

  • Within 24-48h of onset (NOT during first 24h if tPA given). Reduces early recurrence by 7 per 1000 treated (IST, CAST trials).

  • Aspirin + clopidogrel for 21 days for minor stroke (NIHSS <=3) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 >=4) — CHANCE trial. POINT trial extended this to 90 days but bleeding increased after day 21; therefore 21 days remains preferred. Ticagrelor + aspirin (THALES) for 30 days is an alternative.

  • In patients with CYP2C19 loss-of-function (LOF) alleles (common in East Asians — ~60%): ticagrelor + aspirin superior to clopidogrel + aspirin (RRR 23%), since clopidogrel requires CYP2C19 for activation. Consider CYP2C19 genotyping in appropriate patients.

  • Heparin or LMWH not recommended in acute ischemic stroke due to no net benefit and increased hemorrhagic transformation risk (IST, HAEST). Exception: CVT (see 1.10). AF-related stroke: DOACs started 4-14 days post-stroke based on infarct size (1-3-6-12 day rule).

1.9 Malignant MCA Infarction and Decompressive Hemicraniectomy

Malignant MCA infarction (MMCAI) complicates 10-15% of MCA strokes and carries 70-80% mortality without intervention. Clinically defined as: complete MCA territory infarction with progressive cerebral edema causing herniation within 24-96 hours.

DECOMPRESSIVE HEMICRANIECTOMY — KEY CRITERIA AND EVIDENCE
Indications: Space-occupying MCA infarction; progressive deterioration despite medical ICP management
DESTINY II trial (NEJM 2014): Age >60 — surgery reduced mortality (33% vs 70%) but majority of survivors had mRS 4 (severe disability); controversial in older patients
Pooled DECIMAL + DESTINY + HAMLET (NEJM 2007): Age <=60 — NNT=2 for survival; NNT=4 for favorable outcome (mRS <=3)
Timing: Best results within 48h of stroke onset; up to 96h in some cases
AHA 2026 (Class I): Decompressive hemicraniectomy for malignant MCA in patients <= 60 years within 48h with large infarction and decreasing consciousness
AHA 2026 (Class IIb): Consider in patients >60 years after careful shared decision-making
Cerebellar infarction with edema: Suboccipital decompressive craniectomy for deteriorating patients — different anatomy, often better outcomes
Medical bridge therapy: Head-of-bed 30-45 degrees, osmotherapy (mannitol 20% 1g/kg q4-6h; or NaCl 23.4% 30 mL); EVD for acute hydrocephalus; avoid hyperglycemia and hyperthermia

1.10.3 Lipid Management — Updated 2026 Targets

  • Atorvastatin 40-80 mg or rosuvastatin 20-40 mg for ALL ischemic stroke patients regardless of baseline LDL (SPARCL, TST trials).

  • < 55 mg/dL for high-risk patients (atherosclerotic etiology); < 70 mg/dL for others. TST trial (NEJM 2020): LDL <70 vs <100 in patients with prior stroke — reduced recurrence.

  • If LDL not at goal on maximum tolerated statin: add ezetimibe (IMPROVE-IT, SHARP) then PCSK9 inhibitor (evolocumab — FOURIER; alirocumab — ODYSSEY OUTCOMES). PCSK9 inhibitors reduced stroke by 15-16% in ASCVD patients.

  • If TG >500 mg/dL: fibrates (fenofibrate) to prevent pancreatitis. For residual risk with TG 135-500 and LDL at goal: icosapentaenoic acid (IPE/VASCEPA, REDUCE-IT) reduces MACE by 25%; primarily studied in ASCVD patients.

1.10.4 Blood Pressure — Post-Stroke Long-Term

  • <130/80 mmHg for most patients (AHA 2026). More intensive targets may benefit lacunar stroke patients with small vessel disease (SPS3: <130 SBP reduced ICH by 59%).

  • Resume prior antihypertensives at 24-72h. Initiate new therapy after neurological stabilization. Gradual reduction preferred — avoid acute drops.

  • ACEi (perindopril, PROGRESS) + indapamide combination most evidence-based for stroke prevention. ARBs, CCBs also acceptable. Avoid beta-blockers as first-line unless heart failure/AF indication.

The combination is >95% sensitive for brainstem/cerebellar stroke, superior to initial DWI (which may be negative in first 24-48h in brainstem).

1.13.2 Stroke in Pregnancy and Puerperium

  • Stroke risk is 3x higher in pregnancy and 9x higher post-partum (especially first 6 weeks). Incidence 30-80/100,000 deliveries.

  • CVT (puerperium), RCVS (postpartum), peripartum cardiomyopathy, eclampsia/PRES, amniotic fluid embolism, thrombophilia in pregnancy.

  • Class IIb in AHA 2026 — tPA does not cross placenta (large molecule). Risk of maternal hemorrhage must be discussed. Can be considered for life-threatening stroke within tPA window after multidisciplinary discussion with OB and neurology.

  • Class IIa — reasonable if standard eligibility criteria met. Radiation exposure from fluoroscopy is a concern but manageable with shielding.

  • LMWH preferred (does not cross placenta); warfarin teratogenic in first trimester; DOACs contraindicated (pregnancy safety data lacking).

1.13.3 COVID-19-Associated Stroke

  • COVID-19 is associated with a hypercoagulable state due to endothelial injury, platelet activation, cytokine storm, and antiphospholipid antibody formation.

  • Large vessel ischemic stroke (often multiterritory), microvascular thrombosis, CSVT. Occurs in ~1-3% of hospitalized COVID-19 patients.

  • COVID-19 promotes thrombus via endothelial ACE2 receptor binding → IL-6, TNF-alpha, complement activation → prothrombotic milieu.

  • Standard acute stroke management; prophylactic anticoagulation for hospitalized COVID patients (ATTACC, ACTIV-4a); therapeutic anticoagulation if LVO or confirmed thrombosis.

  • Rare complication of adenoviral COVID vaccines (AZ ChAdOx1, J&J Ad26). Anti-PF4 antibodies cause platelet activation and CVT/CSVT. Incidence 1/100,000 doses. Treatment: IVIG, argatroban, fondaparinux — AVOID heparin (worsens platelet activation). Replace with mRNA vaccines.

  • A = longest diameter (cm), B = perpendicular diameter at longest point, C = number of 10-mm CT slices with hemorrhage. Volume = (AxBxC)/2. >30 mL = high-risk; >60 mL = very high-risk.

1.3.4 Surgical Intervention in ICH

  • EMERGENT suboccipital decompressive craniectomy + hematoma evacuation. Strong evidence; potentially life-saving. Do not delay — herniation can occur rapidly.

  • Minimally invasive surgery (parafascicular transsulcal approach) for spontaneous lobar or basal ganglia ICH (30-80 mL): early MIS within 24-72h improved functional outcome at 180 days vs conservative (mRS 0-3: 46% vs 24%). Significant advance over MISTIE III (2019) which used catheter-based aspiration.

  • CT-guided stereotactic aspiration/thrombolysis: reduced hematoma to <15 mL associated with better outcomes, but primary outcome missed significance. Technique refined in ENRICH.

  • STICH, STICH II: conventional craniotomy NOT superior to medical management for most supratentorial ICH. Exceptions: lobar ICH in patients deteriorating within 1 cm of cortical surface (STICH II trend).

1.3.5 Restarting Anticoagulation After ICH

  • Decision requires weighing ongoing thrombotic risk (AF, mechanical valve, prior VTE) vs. re-bleeding risk (CAA, uncontrolled HTN, prior hemorrhage).

  • RESTART trial (Lancet 2019): Antiplatelet restart after ICH — aspirin appears safe (did not increase ICH recurrence; may reduce ischemic events). Limited to antiplatelet, not anticoagulant.

  • Optimal timing unknown. Generally 4-8 weeks post-ICH for small deep hemispheric; 8-12 weeks for lobar (high CAA risk). Ongoing APACHE-AF, ASPIRE, OAC-CPR trials will provide randomized data.

  • Very high re-bleeding risk (3-5%/year). Anticoagulation generally avoided if possible; use aspirin or no antiplatelet. DOAC avoidance if Boston Criteria probable/definite CAA.

  • MRI brain + MR venography is gold standard. CT venography as alternative. CT may show dense triangle sign (sagittal sinus) or empty delta sign (post-contrast). Direct thrombus visualization on T1 (subacute: T1 bright), GRE/SWI (blooming), DWI (restricted if venous infarct present).

  • For large venous infarct with herniation despite medical management. More favorable outcome than arterial malignant infarction due to potentially reversible etiology.

  • Strongly recommended to prevent rebleeding (highest risk in first 24h, 4% per day). ISAT trial: coiling vs clipping — coiling superior for most aneurysms (disability/death 23.5% vs 30.6% at 1 year).

2. Epilepsy and Seizure Disorders

Epilepsy is defined as two or more unprovoked seizures occurring >24 hours apart, OR one unprovoked seizure with a probability of further seizures similar to the general recurrence risk after two unprovoked seizures (>60%), OR diagnosis of an epilepsy syndrome. The 2017 ILAE classification provides the current framework.

2.2 Diagnostic Workup

  • Standard EEG sensitivity is only 50% for interictal epileptiform discharges. Sleep-deprived or prolonged EEG increases yield. Video-EEG monitoring is gold standard for seizure classification and pre-surgical evaluation.

  • MRI brain with epilepsy protocol (thin-cut coronal FLAIR through temporal lobes) for all new-onset epilepsy. CT is acceptable in emergency settings. Look for mesial temporal sclerosis, cortical dysplasia, tumors, vascular malformations.

  • Basic metabolic panel (glucose, Na, Ca, Mg), CBC, LFT, renal function. Toxicology screen if indicated. Prolactin level >2x baseline at 10-20 min post-event supports GTCS (but not sensitive).

CLINICAL PEARL

Mesial temporal sclerosis (MTS) on MRI shows hippocampal atrophy + FLAIR hyperintensity + loss of internal architecture. This is the most common pathological substrate of drug-resistant temporal lobe epilepsy and predicts excellent surgical outcome (Engel Class I in 60-80%).

2.4 Status Epilepticus

Status epilepticus (SE) is defined as continuous seizure activity lasting >5 minutes or two or more seizures without return to baseline. Convulsive SE is a neurological emergency with mortality of 10-20%.

STATUS EPILEPTICUS MANAGEMENT ALGORITHM
STAGE 1 (0-5 min): Stabilize - ABCs, O2, IV access, glucose, thiamine. Check labs.
STAGE 2 (5-20 min): BENZODIAZEPINE - IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg, repeat x1) OR IV diazepam 0.15 mg/kg OR IM midazolam 10 mg (RAMPART trial: IM midazolam non-inferior to IV lorazepam)

CLINICAL PEARL

The ESETT trial (2019) showed IV fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, and valproate are equally effective as second-line agents for benzodiazepine-refractory SE. Choice should be individualized based on patient comorbidities and drug interactions.

2.5 Special Situations

2.5.1 Epilepsy in Pregnancy

  • Pre-conception counseling is essential. Discuss risks of uncontrolled seizures vs. ASM teratogenicity.

  • Folic acid 4-5 mg/day (higher dose than standard) starting pre-conception.

  • Levetiracetam and lamotrigine have the lowest teratogenic risk. Lamotrigine levels drop significantly in pregnancy - monitor monthly and adjust dose.

  • Valproate (highest teratogenicity, dose-dependent NTD risk 1-2%, neurodevelopmental effects). Topiramate (cleft lip/palate risk).

2.5.2 First Seizure Management

  • Single unprovoked seizure: Recurrence risk is 40-50% within 2 years.

  • ASM treatment after first seizure reduces recurrence but does NOT affect long-term remission (MESS, FIRST trials).

  • Treat after first seizure if: abnormal EEG, structural brain lesion, focal neurological deficit, or family history of epilepsy.

  • Driving restrictions: Varies by jurisdiction. Thailand: seizure-free period required. Most US states: 3-12 months seizure-free.

Practice Question 3

A 16-year-old girl presents with episodes of bilateral arm jerking upon awakening, which have occurred intermittently for 6 months. She had one generalized tonic-clonic seizure last week after sleep deprivation. EEG shows 4-6 Hz polyspike-and-wave discharges. Which is the most likely diagnosis and preferred treatment?

  1. A) Childhood absence epilepsy - ethosuximide

  2. B) Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy - valproate or levetiracetam

  3. C) Temporal lobe epilepsy - carbamazepine

  4. D) Lennox-Gastaut syndrome - lamotrigine

Answer: B) Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy - valproate or levetiracetam

Explanation: Morning myoclonic jerks + GTC seizures + polyspike-wave on EEG in an adolescent is classic JME. Valproate is highly effective but should be avoided in women of childbearing age due to teratogenicity; levetiracetam is a good alternative. JME is lifelong - ASM withdrawal leads to high relapse rates.

3. Movement Disorders

Movement disorders are classified as hypokinetic (e.g., parkinsonism) or hyperkinetic (e.g., tremor, chorea, dystonia, myoclonus, tics). This section covers the major conditions relevant to internal medicine and neurology boards.

3.1 Parkinson Disease

3.1.1 Diagnosis

Parkinson disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disease. The UK Brain Bank criteria require bradykinesia plus at least one of: rigidity, 4-6 Hz rest tremor, or postural instability (not caused by visual, vestibular, cerebellar, or proprioceptive dysfunction).

Cardinal Feature Description Clinical Tip
Bradykinesia Slowness of movement with progressive decrement in speed/amplitude Test with finger tapping, hand movements, toe tapping - look for decrement
Rigidity Increased resistance throughout ROM (lead-pipe); cogwheel if superimposed tremor Test at wrist/elbow; augmented by contralateral limb activation (Froment maneuver)
Rest tremor 4-6 Hz, pill-rolling, suppressed by action, enhanced by distraction Present at rest; often asymmetric at onset; re-emergent postural tremor is characteristic
Postural instability Impaired balance reflexes; pull test positive Usually later feature; early postural instability suggests atypical parkinsonism

3.1.2 Non-Motor Features (Often Precede Motor Symptoms)

  • REM sleep behavior disorder (RBD): Strong predictor of alpha-synucleinopathy; 80% convert to PD/DLB

  • Hyposmia/anosmia: Present in >90% of PD; one of earliest features

  • Constipation: Due to enteric nervous system alpha-synuclein pathology

  • Depression and anxiety: Affect up to 40% of PD patients

  • Cognitive decline and dementia (PDD): 80% prevalence with disease duration >20 years

  • Autonomic dysfunction: Orthostatic hypotension, urinary urgency, erectile dysfunction

Levodopa does NOT cause PD progression (LEAP trial, ELLDOPA). The motor complications (wearing-off, dyskinesia) are related to disease progression and pulsatile dopaminergic stimulation, not levodopa toxicity. Do not delay levodopa when patients need it for functional independence.

  • STN or GPi targets. For motor fluctuations despite optimal medical therapy. Best candidates: clear levodopa response, no significant cognitive impairment, age <70.

  • Continuous jejunal infusion via PEG-J. For severe motor fluctuations.

  • Rescue injection for acute off periods. Continuous infusion available in some countries.

CLINICAL PEARL

NEVER give typical antipsychotics (haloperidol) to patients with DLB or PD. Neuroleptic sensitivity is a core feature of DLB and can cause life-threatening rigidity, confusion, and autonomic instability. If antipsychotic is needed, use quetiapine (lowest D2 affinity) or pimavanserin (selective 5-HT2A inverse agonist, no D2 blockade).

3.3 Essential Tremor

  • Most common movement disorder (prevalence 1-5%). Bilateral action/postural tremor (6-12 Hz), often improves with alcohol.

  • First-line: propranolol (60-320 mg/day) or primidone (25-750 mg/day). Second-line: topiramate, gabapentin. Refractory: DBS (VIM thalamus) or focused ultrasound thalamotomy.

3.4 Chorea

  • Autosomal dominant, CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion (>36 repeats) in HTT gene. Progressive chorea, behavioral changes, cognitive decline. Treatment: tetrabenazine or deutetrabenazine (VMAT2 inhibitors) for chorea.

  • Post-streptococcal (rheumatic fever); most common cause of chorea in children. Self-limited; penicillin prophylaxis.

  • Levodopa (dyskinesia), neuroleptics (tardive dyskinesia), OCP, phenytoin.

  • Repetitive orolingual movements from chronic dopamine receptor blockade. Treatment: VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine, deutetrabenazine).

3.5 Dystonia

  • Sustained or intermittent muscle contractions causing abnormal postures/movements. Classified by age, distribution, temporal pattern, and etiology.

  • Most common focal dystonia in adults. Treatment: botulinum toxin injection (first-line).

  • DYT1 (TOR1A gene) most common inherited form. Trial of levodopa essential to rule out dopa-responsive dystonia (DYT5/DRD - dramatic sustained response).

  • Botulinum toxin for focal; anticholinergics (trihexyphenidyl) for generalized; baclofen; DBS (GPi) for refractory generalized dystonia.

Headache is one of the most common neurological complaints. The ICHD-3 (International Classification of Headache Disorders, 3rd edition) provides the diagnostic framework. Primary headaches (migraine, tension-type, cluster) must be distinguished from secondary headaches that may indicate life-threatening pathology.

4.2 Migraine

4.2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (ICHD-3)

Migraine without aura requires at least 5 attacks fulfilling: headache lasting 4-72 hours, at least 2 of (unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, aggravated by routine physical activity), and at least 1 of (nausea/vomiting, photophobia AND phonophobia).

Migraine with aura requires at least 2 attacks with fully reversible visual, sensory, or speech/language symptoms. Aura develops over 5-60 minutes, each symptom lasts 5-60 minutes.

4.3 Cluster Headache

  • Severe unilateral orbital/periorbital pain lasting 15-180 minutes, with ipsilateral autonomic features (lacrimation, conjunctival injection, rhinorrhea, ptosis/miosis, eyelid edema).

  • Occurs in clusters: 1-8 attacks/day for weeks to months, with remission periods.

  • High-flow O2 (12-15 L/min via non-rebreather for 15-20 min) - 78% effective. Sumatriptan 6 mg SC. Intranasal zolmitriptan.

  • Verapamil (first-line, up to 960 mg/day - requires ECG monitoring for heart block). Short-term bridge with prednisone or suboccipital steroid injection. Galcanezumab approved for episodic cluster headache.

5. Neuromuscular Disorders

5.1 Myasthenia Gravis (MG)

Autoimmune disorder of the neuromuscular junction characterized by fatigable weakness. Bimodal age distribution: young women (20-40) and older men (60-80).

5.1.1 Clinical Features

  • Fluctuating weakness that worsens with activity and improves with rest

  • Ocular MG (50% at presentation): ptosis, diplopia. ~50% progress to generalized MG within 2 years

  • Generalized MG: proximal limb weakness, bulbar symptoms (dysarthria, dysphagia, fatigable chewing), respiratory weakness

  • NO sensory involvement, NO pain, NO reflex changes (pure motor)

5.1.2 Diagnosis

Test Sensitivity Notes

5.1.3 Treatment

  • Pyridostigmine (cholinesterase inhibitor) 60 mg TID, titrate to effect. Caution with MuSK-MG (often ineffective/worsening).

  • Prednisone (start low, titrate up to avoid initial worsening). Azathioprine, mycophenolate as steroid-sparing agents. Rituximab especially effective in MuSK-MG.

  • Recommended for all thymoma patients. MGTX trial: benefit in non-thymomatous AChR+ generalized MG age 18-65.

  • IVIg (0.4 g/kg x 5 days) or plasma exchange (PLEX, 5-7 sessions). Equal efficacy.

  • Eculizumab, ravulizumab, zilucoplan for refractory AChR+ generalized MG.

  • Efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab - reduce pathogenic IgG levels; rapid onset.

MYASTHENIC CRISIS
Life-threatening respiratory failure from MG exacerbation
Triggers: infection, surgery, medication changes, non-compliance, drugs (aminoglycosides, beta-blockers, Mg)

5.2 Guillain-Barre Syndrome (GBS)

Acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (AIDP) is the most common form. Post-infectious autoimmune disorder; preceded by GI (Campylobacter jejuni) or respiratory infection in 60-70%.

5.2.1 Clinical Features

  • Ascending symmetric weakness (legs before arms), areflexia/hyporeflexia

  • Sensory symptoms (paresthesias) often precede weakness but motor predominates

  • Facial weakness (bilateral) in 50%; bulbar weakness; autonomic dysfunction (tachycardia, BP lability, urinary retention)

  • Respiratory failure in 25-30% - monitor FVC and NIF closely

  • Peak severity within 4 weeks (monophasic); nadir usually at 2-3 weeks

  • Albuminocytologic dissociation (elevated protein with normal cell count). May be normal in first week.

  • Serial FVC and NIF measurements. Intubate if FVC <20 mL/kg, NIF < -30 cmH2O, or >30% decline. DVT prophylaxis. Pain management.

6. Demyelinating Diseases

6.1 Multiple Sclerosis

MS is the most common demyelinating disease of the CNS, affecting approximately 2.8 million people worldwide. It predominantly affects young women (F:M ratio 3:1), with onset typically between ages 20-40.

6.1.1 Clinical Subtypes

Subtype Frequency Course Key Features
Relapsing-remitting (RRMS) 85% Discrete attacks with full/partial recovery Most common at onset; attacks last days to weeks
Secondary progressive (SPMS) ~50% of RRMS Gradual worsening after initial relapsing course Develops 15-20 years after RRMS onset
Primary progressive (PPMS) 10-15% Gradual progression from onset without relapses More common in men, older onset, spinal cord predominant
Clinically isolated syndrome (CIS) N/A First clinical episode suggestive of MS Conversion to MS depends on MRI lesion burden

6.1.2 Common Clinical Presentations

  • Unilateral painful vision loss (pain with eye movement), afferent pupillary defect (RAPD), central scotoma. Most common presenting symptom of MS.

  • Motor/sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction. MS lesions are partial/asymmetric and span <2 vertebral segments (vs. NMOSD which is longitudinally extensive >= 3 segments).

  • Internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO) - highly suggestive of MS in young adults. Trigeminal neuralgia in young patients. Vertigo, diplopia.

  • Electric shock sensation down spine/limbs with neck flexion. Indicates dorsal column cervical cord demyelination.

  • Worsening of symptoms with heat/exercise. Due to temperature-dependent conduction block in demyelinated axons.

6.1.3 Diagnosis (McDonald Criteria 2017)

Requires demonstration of dissemination in space (DIS) and dissemination in time (DIT), with exclusion of alternative diagnoses.

  • >= 1 T2 lesion in >= 2 of 4 characteristic locations: periventricular, cortical/juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord.

  • Simultaneous gadolinium-enhancing and non-enhancing lesions on single MRI, OR new T2/enhancing lesion on follow-up MRI.

  • Oligoclonal bands (OCBs) present in >95% of MS. Can substitute for DIT in 2017 criteria. IgG index elevated.

CLINICAL PEARL

Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h for 4 days should be given WITH or BEFORE the first dose of antibiotics in suspected bacterial meningitis (not after). The benefit is primarily for S. pneumoniae meningitis, reducing mortality and neurological sequelae (de Gans trial). Give before or with antibiotics - if antibiotics already given, benefit is reduced.

7.1.3 When to CT Before LP

  • Immunocompromised state

  • History of CNS disease (mass, stroke, focal infection)

  • New-onset seizure (within 1 week)

  • Papilledema

  • Altered consciousness

  • Focal neurological deficit

CRITICAL: Do NOT delay antibiotics while waiting for CT. Draw blood cultures and start empiric antibiotics + dexamethasone immediately, then proceed with imaging and LP.

5.2 Viral Encephalitis

HSV encephalitis is the most common cause of sporadic fatal encephalitis. It has a predilection for the temporal lobes.

  • Fever, headache, altered consciousness, behavioral changes, seizures, focal deficits. MRI: temporal lobe hyperintensity. CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, HSV PCR positive. Treatment: IV acyclovir 10 mg/kg q8h for 14-21 days. Start empirically - do NOT wait for PCR results.

  • Most common vaccine-preventable cause of encephalitis in Asia. Thalamic/basal ganglia involvement on MRI. Supportive care only; prevention by vaccination.

  • NMDA-receptor encephalitis: young women, psychiatric symptoms, seizures, movement disorder, autonomic instability. Associated with ovarian teratoma. CSF anti-NMDAR antibodies. Treatment: immunotherapy (steroids, IVIg, plasma exchange) + tumor removal if present.

5.3 Tuberculous Meningitis

TBM is the most lethal form of TB. Common in Thailand and other endemic regions. Presents subacutely with headache, fever, cranial nerve palsies (especially CN VI), and altered consciousness over days to weeks.

  • CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis, very high protein, very low glucose. AFB smear sensitivity only 10-20%. GeneXpert MTB/RIF on CSF improves rapid diagnosis. ADA >10 U/L supportive.

  • RHZE for 2 months + RH for 10 months (total 12 months). Adjunctive dexamethasone for all patients (Thwaites trial: reduces mortality). Address hydrocephalus (VP shunt if needed).

7.4 Cryptococcal Meningitis

Most common in HIV/AIDS (CD4 <100). Also seen in solid organ transplant recipients and patients on immunosuppressive therapy.

  • Serum and CSF cryptococcal antigen (CrAg) - sensitivity >95%. CSF India ink positive in 70-80%. Very high opening pressure is characteristic.

  • Induction: amphotericin B desoxycolate (0.7-1 mg/kg/day) + flucytosine for 2 weeks. Consolidation: fluconazole 400 mg/day for 8 weeks. Maintenance: fluconazole 200 mg/day for >= 1 year. Aggressive ICP management with serial LPs or lumbar drain.

Practice Question 7

A 35-year-old HIV-positive man (CD4 count 50 cells/uL) presents with 2 weeks of headache and confusion. CSF shows: WBC 20 (100% lymphocytes), protein 80 mg/dL, glucose 30 mg/dL, opening pressure 350 mm H2O. What is the most important initial diagnostic test?

  1. A) CSF Gram stain and culture

  2. B) CSF cryptococcal antigen (CrAg)

  3. C) CSF HSV PCR

  4. D) CSF VDRL

Answer: B) CSF cryptococcal antigen (CrAg)

Explanation: HIV-positive patient with very low CD4, subacute headache/confusion, lymphocytic CSF with low glucose, and markedly elevated opening pressure is classic for cryptococcal meningitis. CrAg has >95% sensitivity and is the fastest reliable test. The very high opening pressure is a hallmark of cryptococcal meningitis and is the primary cause of mortality - aggressive pressure management with serial LPs is critical.

8.3 Treatment

  • Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine. For mild-moderate AD and DLB. Modest symptomatic benefit. GI side effects, bradycardia.

Practice Question 9

A 72-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of progressive gait difficulty (magnetic gait), followed by urinary urgency and mild memory impairment. MRI shows ventriculomegaly out of proportion to sulcal enlargement. What is the most appropriate diagnostic test?

  1. A) Lumbar puncture with high-volume CSF tap test

  2. B) PET amyloid imaging

  3. C) EEG

  4. D) Genetic testing for MAPT mutation

Answer: A) Lumbar puncture with high-volume CSF tap test

Explanation: This triad of gait apraxia (magnetic gait), urinary incontinence, and dementia with ventriculomegaly is classic for normal pressure hydrocephalus (NPH). A large-volume CSF tap test (30-50 mL) with pre/post gait assessment is both diagnostic and prognostic - improvement predicts response to VP shunt placement. Gait is the most responsive symptom to treatment.

10.2 REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD)

  • Dream enactment behavior during REM sleep (loss of normal REM atonia)

  • >80% of idiopathic RBD convert to alpha-synucleinopathy (PD, DLB, MSA) within 10-15 years. This is a prodromal marker.

  • Melatonin (first-line, 3-12 mg at bedtime) or clonazepam (0.5-2 mg). Bedroom safety measures.

10.3 Restless Legs Syndrome

  • Urge to move legs (with unpleasant sensations), worse at rest, worse in evening/night, relieved by movement

  • Check serum ferritin. If <75 mcg/L, supplement iron (target ferritin >100 mcg/L). Iron deficiency is the most common secondary cause.

  • First-line: alpha-2-delta ligands (gabapentin, pregabalin). Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole) effective but risk of augmentation with long-term use. Avoid: antihistamines, SSRIs/SNRIs, dopamine antagonists (all worsen RLS).

11.2 Brain Metastases

  • Most common intracranial tumors overall (10x more common than primary brain tumors)

  • Lung (most common), breast, melanoma, renal, colorectal

  • Single metastasis: surgical resection + SRS or whole-brain radiation. Multiple: SRS (if limited number) or WBRT. Immunotherapy increasingly used (especially melanoma, lung).

CLINICAL PEARL

If a patient presents with a ring-enhancing brain lesion, the differential includes GBM, brain abscess, metastasis, and lymphoma. Key distinguishing features: GBM has irregular thick ring with central necrosis; abscess has smooth thin ring with restricted diffusion on DWI; lymphoma is homogeneously enhancing and periventricular; metastasis often at gray-white junction.

12.2 Key Etiologies

  • Cervical spondylotic myelopathy (most common), epidural abscess (emergency: fever + back pain + neurological deficit = MRI + surgical decompression), metastatic epidural compression (dexamethasone + urgent radiation/surgery).

  • MS, NMOSD (LETM), sarcoidosis, SLE. MRI: T2 hyperintensity with/without enhancement.

  • Anterior spinal artery syndrome (post-aortic surgery). Spinal dural AVF: progressive myelopathy in older men; flow voids on MRI.

  • TB (Pott disease), HIV vacuolar myelopathy, HTLV-1 associated myelopathy, schistosomiasis.

  • B12 deficiency (subacute combined degeneration), copper deficiency (zinc toxicity, post-bariatric surgery).

CLINICAL PEARL

Spinal epidural abscess is a neurosurgical emergency. The classic triad is fever, back pain, and neurological deficit, but all three are present in only 10-15% at initial presentation. Have a low threshold for MRI in patients with back pain and fever, especially with risk factors (IVDU, diabetes, recent spinal procedure, immunosuppression). Delayed diagnosis leads to irreversible paralysis.
