## Executive Summary

Factor XIa (FXIa) inhibitors represent a paradigm shift in anticoagulation — targeting the intrinsic coagulation pathway with the promise of **equivalent efficacy with superior safety** compared to standard DOACs. Two major drug candidates have emerged:

- **Abelacimab** (Anthos Pharmaceuticals) — monoclonal antibody, IV q90d
- **Asundexian** (Bayer) — small molecule oral agent

The 2024-2025 trial results tell a **split story**: abelacimab showed a stunning **67% reduction in major bleeding** vs rivaroxaban, while asundexian **failed to meet noninferiority** vs apixaban in OCEANIC-AF.

---

## Background: Why FXIa?

### Coagulation Cascade — The Traditional View

Extrinsic pathway (Factor VIIa → Factor Xa → Factor IIa/thrombin) is the classic target of warfarin and DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran).

### FXIa — The Intrinsic Pathway

| Feature | FXIa Inhibition | Factor Xa Inhibition |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Role | Intrinsic pathway amplification | Common pathway terminal step |
| Thrombus propagation | Yes — FXIa sustains amplification | Direct thrombin generation |
| Bleeding risk | Potentially lower (upstream target) | Higher |
| Oral options | Emerging | DOACs available |

**Rationale:** FXIa is upstream of Factor Xa, primarily involved in **amplification** of coagulation (propagation phase), not initiation. This may provide sufficient anticoagulation for stroke prevention while reducing bleeding risk.

## Drug Profiles

### 1. Abelacimab — AZALEA-TIMI 71 Phase 2 Trial

**Full title:** *"Abelacimab vs Rivaroxaban in Atrial Fibrillation"*

**Reference:** Giugliano RP et al. NEJM. October 2024. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa2406674

#### Mechanism
- **Type:** Fully human monoclonal antibody (IgG1)
- **Target:** Factor XIa (Kunitz-type protease inhibitor)
- **Administration:** IV infusion every 90 days (subcutaneous formulation in development)
- **Half-life:** Extended — allows infrequent dosing
- **Binding:** Covalent binding to FXIa active site — irreversible inhibition

#### Study Design

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Design | Randomized, open-label, blinded endpoint (PROBE) |
| Population | Atrial fibrillation, moderate-to-high stroke risk (CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 M / ≥3 F) |
| Sample | 1,287 patients |
| Arms | 3 arms: Abelacimab 90mg IV q90d vs 150mg IV q90d vs Rivaroxaban 20mg QD oral |
| Duration | 12 months |
| Primary endpoint | Major or clinically relevant nonmajor bleeding |
| Secondary endpoint | Composite of cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism |

#### Results

**Primary Safety Endpoint — Annualized Rates:**

| Outcome | Abelacimab 90mg | Abelacimab 150mg | Rivaroxaban | P-value |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Major bleeding** | 0.65% | 0.47% | 1.99% | HR 0.33 (67% reduction) |
| CRNM bleeding | 2.47% | 2.41% | 5.59% | HR 0.44 |
| **Any bleeding** | 4.27% | 3.71% | 8.32% | HR 0.51 |

**Efficacy Endpoint — Annualized Rates:**

| Outcome | Abelacimab (pooled) | Rivaroxaban |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| CV death / stroke / SEE | 1.57% | 1.22% |
| Stroke / SEE | 1.23% | 0.77% |

⚠️ **Important caveat:** Abelacimab numerically had *higher* rates of thrombotic events (not statistically significant, trial not powered for efficacy).

#### Key Takeaways — AZALEA-TIMI 71

✅ **Major bleeding reduced by 67%** vs rivaroxaban — unprecedented safety signal
✅ Single IV infusion every 90 days — potential for improved adherence
✅ Comparable thrombotic event rates — trend favors rivaroxaban but NS
⚠️ Open-label design, Phase 2 — needs Phase 3 confirmation
⚠️ Infusion reactions, IV administration burden
⚠️ Monthly visits required (current formulation)

#### Ongoing Trials

**LILAC-TIMI 76 Phase 3** (NCT05643573)
- ~9,000 patients with atrial fibrillation
- Primary endpoint: Stroke or systemic embolism (noninferiority vs DOAC)
- Primary safety: Major bleeding
- Expected results: 2025-2026

### 2. Asundexian — OCEANIC-AF Phase 3 Trial

**Reference:** Piccini JP et al. NEJM. 2024. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa2407105

#### Mechanism
- **Type:** Small molecule (non-covalent)
- **Target:** FXIa active site
- **Administration:** Oral once daily
- **Half-life:** ~12-14 hours — allows once-daily oral dosing
- **Advantage over abelacimab:** Fully oral, no infusion required

#### OCEANIC-AF Study Design

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Design | Randomized, double-blind, double-dummy, event-driven |
| Population | Atrial fibrillation, moderate-to-high stroke risk |
| Sample | ~13,000+ patients planned; trial stopped early |
| Arms | Asundexian 50mg QD vs Apixaban 5mg BID |
| Primary endpoint | Composite of stroke or systemic embolism (noninferiority margin 1.375) |
| Secondary safety | Major bleeding (ISTH) |

#### Results

| Outcome | Asundexian | Apixaban | HR (95% CI) |
|---------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Stroke / SEE (primary) | 1.78%/yr | 1.14%/yr | 1.57 (1.12–2.19) |
| Major bleeding | 0.88%/yr | 1.17%/yr | 0.75 (0.54–1.04) |
| CV death | — | — | Similar |

**❌ Trial stopped early** — asundexian did NOT meet noninferiority criteria for stroke/SEE vs apixaban
- Stroke rate numerically higher in asundexian arm
- Bleeding rate lower (safety signal consistent with mechanism)
- BUT inferior efficacy = net harm

#### Why Did Asundexian Fail?

**Hypotheses:**

1. **Insufficient FXIa inhibition** — plasma inhibition may not have been adequate for cardioembolic stroke prevention
2. **Factor Xa compensation** — when FXIa is blocked, Factor Xa pathway may compensate via extrinsic route
3. **Patient population** — high-risk AF patients may require more complete anticoagulation than partial FXIa inhibition can provide
4. **Trial power** — early termination reduced statistical power

#### PACIFIC-Stroke Trial

- Secondary stroke prevention in non-cardioembolic stroke / TIA
- Asundexian vs aspirin
- **Result:** No significant reduction in recurrent stroke
- Reinforces concerns about FXIa monotherapy efficacy in stroke prevention

#### Key Takeaways — Asundexian/OCEANIC-AF

❌ **Failed to prove noninferiority** — cannot replace DOACs in AF
⚠️ Bleeding signal consistent with mechanism (0.75 HR for major bleeding)
⚠️ Oral route better for adherence, but efficacy insufficient
❌ Development program likely to be discontinued or restructured

## Clinical Implications 2026

### Current Standard of Care

| DOAC | Dose | Advantage | Limitation |
|------|------|-----------|------------|
| Apixaban | 5mg BID | Best bleeding profile in RCTs | BID dosing |
| Rivaroxaban | 20mg QD | Once daily | Bleeding risk |
| Edoxaban | 60mg QD | Liver metabolism | Less data |
| Dabigatran | 150mg BID | Reversible | BID, GI effects |

### Where Do FXIa Inhibitors Fit?

**Abelacimab — Potential Role:**
- Patients with high bleeding risk (HAS-BLED ≥3) who need anticoagulation but cannot tolerate DOAC bleeding
- Patients with demonstrated DOAC-related major bleeding
- Once 90-day subcutaneous formulation available — potential for high adherence
- **NOT YET APPROVED** — awaiting LILAC-TIMI 76 Phase 3 results

**Asundexian:**
- Clinical development uncertain after OCEANIC-AF failure
- May still have niche role in combination therapy or specific populations
- Not recommended for AF stroke prevention at this time

---

## Limitations & Future Directions

### Current Evidence Gaps

1. **Phase 3 data for abelacimab** — LILAC-TIMI 76 ongoing, results pending
2. **Long-term safety** — bleeding signal impressive in 12-month Phase 2, but long-term immunogenicity, infusion reactions unknown
3. **Reversal agent** — no specific reversal agent for abelacimab in case of life-threatening bleeding (andexanet alfa does not reverse it)
4. **Head-to-head vs DOAC efficacy** — abelacimab trend numerically worse, needs Phase 3 powered comparison

### Future Directions

- **Subcutaneous abelacimab formulation** — more practical than IV for chronic use
- **Combination therapy** — FXIa + antiplatelet for dual pathway inhibition
- **Specific populations** — cancer-associated VTE, prosthetic valve (TBD)

## Conclusion

| | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Abelacimab** | ⭐ Promising — 67% bleeding reduction is unprecedented; awaiting Phase 3 (LILAC-TIMI 76) |
| **Asundexian** | ❌ Failed — cannot recommend at this time; efficacy concern outweighs bleeding benefit |
| **FXIa hypothesis** | 🔬 Valid but complex — mechanism works in animal models, translation to human AF uncertain |

**Bottom line:** Abelacimab represents the most exciting anticoagulant safety development in years. If Phase 3 confirms the bleeding advantage while maintaining efficacy, it could transform anticoagulation in high-risk AF patients. Asundexian's failure is a cautionary tale about translating coagulation mechanisms from preclinical to clinical.
