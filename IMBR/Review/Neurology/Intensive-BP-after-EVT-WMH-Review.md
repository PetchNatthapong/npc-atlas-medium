## 1. Executive Summary

The post-thrombectomy period represents a critical window for blood pressure (BP) optimization, with competing priorities: maintaining adequate cerebral perfusion pressure in the newly reperfused territory while avoiding hyperperfusion injury, hemorrhage transformation, and sustained elevation that may worsen edema. The **OPTIMAL-BP trial** challenged the assumption that intensive BP lowering is universally beneficial following successful endovascular thrombectomy (EVT), demonstrating that targeting systolic BP <140 mmHg within 24 hours worsened functional outcomes compared to conventional management (140–180 mmHg).

A landmark **secondary analysis** of the OPTIMAL-BP trial specifically examined how pre-existing white matter hyperintensity (WMH) severity modifies the relationship between post-EVT BP management and clinical outcomes. In **271 patients** stratified by WMH burden (Fazekas score ≥3 defining severe WMH), the study revealed a critical heterogeneity of treatment effect:

| WMH Severity | Intensive BP (<140 mmHg) | Conventional BP (140–180 mmHg) | Outcome |
|--------------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| **Severe WMH** (Fazekas ≥3) | 29.9% mRS 0–2 | 51.3% mRS 0–2 | **Intensive BP significantly worse** |
| **Mild WMH** (Fazekas <3) | No significant difference | — | **NS between groups** |

**Key Conclusions:**
1. **Severe WMH identifies a vulnerable subgroup** in which intensive BP lowering is harmful
2. **"One size does not fit all"** — uniform intensive BP targets post-EVT are not appropriate
3. **Cerebral autoregulation impairment** in severe WMH explains the observed harm
4. **Individualized BP targets** based on baseline brain health are warranted
5. **Clinical implication:** Avoid intensive BP lowering (<140 mmHg) in patients with severe WMH post-EVT

## 2. Background: Stroke, Endovascular Thrombectomy, and Blood Pressure Management

### 2.1 Acute Ischemic Stroke and Large Vessel Occlusion

Acute ischemic stroke (AIS) due to large vessel occlusion (LVO) represents the most severe stroke phenotype, accounting for approximately 30–40% of all ischemic strokes but disproportionately contributing to mortality, disability, and healthcare burden. The paradigm shift in LVO management occurred with the publication of multiple landmark EVT trials (MR CLEAN, EXTEND-IA, SWIFT PRIME, REVASCAT, ESCAPE) demonstrating that rapid mechanical thrombectomy achieving successful reperfusion (TICI 2b–3) significantly improves functional outcomes when performed within 6–24 hours of symptom onset.

**Epidemiology of LVO:**
- Incidence: ~30–40 per 100,000 population annually
- Mortality without treatment: 20–30% at 90 days
- With EVT: Mortality reduced to 10–15%; functional independence (mRS 0–2) achieved in 46–51%

### 2.2 Endovascular Thrombectomy: Technical Considerations

**EVT Modalities:**
| Technique | Mechanism | Reperfusion Rate |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Stent retrievers (Solitaire, Trevo) | Mechanical clot extraction | TICI 2b–3: 59–71% |
| aspiration catheter (ADAPT) | Direct aspiration first | TICI 2b–3: 52–82% |
| Combined (Solumbra) | Stentriever + aspiration | TICI 2b–3: 70–88% |

**Time is Brain:**
- Estimated 1.9 million neurons lost per minute in LVO
- Door-to-groin time: median 60–90 minutes in experienced centers
- Successful reperfusion: every 30-minute delay reduces mRS 0–2 by 6–8%

### 2.3 Blood Pressure Management Post-EVT: The Clinical Challenge

The immediate post-EVT period presents a complex hemodynamic challenge with competing priorities:

**Pathophysiologic Considerations:**

| Factor | Effect | BP Management Implication |
|--------|--------|-------------------------|
| **Ischemic core** | Neuronal death, release of DAMPs | Need adequate perfusion pressure |
| **Penumbra** | Hypoperfused but viable tissue | Risk of extension if BP too low |
| **Reperffusion injury** | Oxidative stress, inflammation | Suggests BP moderation |
| **Blood-brain barrier disruption** | Edema, hemorrhage risk | Suggests BP control |
| **Chronic small vessel disease** | Impaired autoregulation | Variable response to BP changes |
| **Collateral circulation** | Variable compensatory flow | Patient-specific BP needs |

**The Autoregulation Curve:**

```
Cerebral Blood Flow (CBF)
    ↑
    │                    Autoregulation
    │                ┌────────────────────┐
    │                │                    │
    │   ─────────────┤                    ├──────────
    │ Normal MAP     │                    │   Critical
    │ range          │                    │   Lowering
    │ (60-150 mmHg) │                    │
    │                └────────────────────┘
    └────────────────────────────────────────────────→ MAP
         ↓CBF        Optimal Zone        ↑CBF/Edema
        (Ischemia)                     (Hyperemia)
```

**Traditional BP Targets Post-EVT:**

| Guideline | Systolic BP Target | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **AHA/ASA 2019** | <180/105 mmHg | Expert consensus; limited RCT data |
| **ESO 2021** | <180 mmHg | Prevents hemorrhage; maintains perfusion |
| **Current practice** | Variable (140–180 mmHg) | Wide variation exists |

### 2.4 The OPTIMAL-BP Trial: Challenging Uniform Intensive Targets

**Trial Design:**
- Multicenter, randomized, open-label, blinded endpoint assessment (PROBE)
- 19 stroke centers in South Korea
- June 2020 – November 2022
- n=307 patients with LVO, successful EVT (TICI 2b–3), SBP ≥140 mmHg within 2 hours

**Randomization:**
| Arm | Target Systolic BP | Duration |
|-----|-------------------|----------|
| **Intensive** | <140 mmHg | First 24 hours |
| **Conventional** | 140–180 mmHg | First 24 hours |

**Results (Primary Analysis — STOPPED EARLY):**

| Outcome | Intensive (n=154) | Conventional (n=153) | P-value |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|---------|
| **mRS 0–2 at 90 days** | 39.4% | 54.4% | P = .02 |
| mRS shift (ordinal) | Worse with intensive | Better with conventional | OR 1.64 |
| Symptomatic ICH | 9.1% | 4.0% | P = .08 |
| Mortality | 17.5% | 8.5% | P = .03 |

**Trial stopped early by DSMB for safety concerns.**

## 3. White Matter Hyperintensity: Pathophysiology and Clinical Significance

### 3.1 Definition and Imaging Characteristics

White matter hyperintensities (WMHs) are areas of increased signal intensity on T2-weighted and FLAIR MRI sequences within the cerebral white matter. They represent histopathologically:

- **Demyelination** and axonal loss
- **Gliosis** (reactive astrocytosis)
- **Vacuolization** and rarefaction of white matter
- **Small vessel wall thickening** and lipohyalinosis

**Clinical significance:**
- Neuroimaging marker of **cerebral small vessel disease (CSVD)**
- Associated with: cognitive decline, gait disturbance, depression, stroke risk
- Predicts: post-stroke outcomes, recurrence, mortality

### 3.2 The Fazekas Scale: Grading WMH Severity

The Fazekas scale is the most widely validated and used visual rating system for WMH burden:

**Periventricular WMH (PVH) Scoring:**

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **0** | No periventricular lesions |
| **1** | "Caps" or pencil-thin lining |
| **2** | Smooth "halo" or irregular periventricular signal |
| **3** | Irregular periventricular signal extending into deep white matter |

**Deep White Matter WMH (DWMH) Scoring:**

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **0** | No deep white matter lesions |
| **1** | Punctate foci (≤5 mm) |
| **2** | Beginning confluence (6–10 mm, 3+ lesions) |
| **3** | Large confluent lesions (>10 mm, extensive) |

**Total Fazekas Score:** PVH + DWMH = 0–6  
**Severe WMH Definition:** Total Fazekas score ≥3

**Population Prevalence:**
- Mild WMH: ~50% of stroke-free elderly
- Severe WMH (Fazekas ≥3): ~10–20% of stroke-free elderly; up to 60% in acute stroke populations

### 3.3 Cerebral Small Vessel Disease: The Underlying Pathology

CSVD refers to pathological processes affecting the small vessels of the brain:

| Vessel Type | Size | Pathology | Imaging Findings |
|-------------|------|-----------|------------------|
| **Lipohyalinotic arterioles** | 100–400 μm | Wall thickening, fibrinoid necrosis | WMH, lacunes |
| **Amyloid angiopathy** | 50–200 μm | Aβ deposition, wall weakening | WMH, microbleeds, lobar ICH |
| **Atherosclerotic** | 400–800 μm | Plaque, stenosis | WMH, territorial infarcts |

**Mechanisms of WMH Formation:**

```
Chronic Hypertension / Aging
         ↓
  Small Vessel Wall Thickening
  (Lipohyalinosis, Fibrinoid Necrosis)
         ↓
  Reduced Cerebral Blood Flow
         ↓
  Ischemia of White Matter
  (Deep Periventricular Territory)
         ↓
  Oligodendrocyte Death
  Myelin Degradation
         ↓
  WHITE MATTER HYPERINTENSITIES
```

### 3.4 Cerebral Autoregulation in CSVD

Cerebral autoregulation maintains constant CBF across a wide MAP range (typically 60–150 mmHg). This mechanism is mediated by:

- **Myogenic response:** Vascular smooth muscle constriction/dilation in response to pressure changes
- **Metabolic factors:** Local metabolites ( adenosine, K+, H+) modulate vessel tone
- **Neurogenic factors:** Sympathetic innervation

**Autoregulation Impairment in CSVD:**

| Normal | CSVD State |
|--------|-----------|
| Wide autoregulatory plateau | Right-shifted, narrowed plateau |
| Stable CBF 60–150 mmHg MAP | CBF varies with BP changes |
| Protected perfusion | Vulnerable to hypoperfusion |

**Right-Shifted Autoregulation:**

```
CBF
 ↑
 │    Normal         CSVD (Impaired)
 │   ┌──────┐      ┌────────┐
 │   │      │      │        │
 │───│      │───→──┘        └─────
 │   │      │      ↑          ↑
 │   └──────┘   Higher       CBF
 │  60-150   MAP needed     varies
 └──────────────────────────────────→ MAP
```

**Clinical Implication:**
In patients with severe WMH/CSVD:
- Higher MAP required to maintain adequate CBF
- Aggressive BP lowering → hypoperfusion of vulnerable white matter
- Even moderate hypotension can cause ischemia in "oligemic" areas

## 4. OPTIMAL-BP WMH Secondary Analysis: Detailed Results

### 4.1 Study Design and Population

**Design:** Pre-specified secondary analysis of the OPTIMAL-BP trial  
**Population:** n=271 patients with available baseline MRI and WMH assessment

**Baseline Characteristics:**

| Characteristic | Severe WMH (n=163) | Mild WMH (n=108) |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Age, mean ± SD | 68.2 ± 10.1 | 65.4 ± 11.3 |

### 4.3 Secondary Outcomes

| Outcome | Severe WMH | Mild WMH |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **mRS shift (ordinal)** | Intensive worse (aOR 2.1, 95% CI 1.2–3.8) | No significant difference |
| **90-day mortality** | 22.5% vs 11.0% (intensive vs conventional) | No significant difference |
| **Symptomatic ICH** | 12.5% vs 4.9% (intensive vs conventional) | No significant difference |
| **Excellent outcome (mRS 0–1)** | 15.4% vs 29.6% (intensive vs conventional) | No significant difference |

### 4.4 Subgroup Analysis Within Severe WMH

**Fazekas Score 3 vs 4–6:**
- Similar direction of effect
- Trend toward greater harm with higher Fazekas scores
- Limited power for formal statistical testing

**Baseline NIHSS Stratification:**
- Effect consistent across NIHSS severity levels
- Trend: greater absolute harm in severe strokes (high NIHSS)

### 4.5 Mechanistic Interpretation

**Why does intensive BP lowering harm patients with severe WMH?**

| Mechanism | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Right-shifted autoregulation** | Higher BP required to maintain CBF in damaged white matter |
| **Oligemic penumbra extension** | Aggressive BP lowering reduces perfusion in watershed zones |
| **Impaired collateral response** | Severely affected white matter has reduced collateral capacity |
| **Chronic hypoperfusion** | WMH tissue adapted to higher baseline MAP |
| **Blood-brain barrier vulnerability** | CSVD causes fragile microvasculature prone to underperfusion |

**Pathophysiologic Model:**

```
SEVERE WMH + INTENSIVE BP LOWERING
         ↓
Impaired Autoregulation
(No compensatory vasodilation)
         ↓
Reduced Cerebral Blood Flow in White Matter
(Vulnerable to BP changes)
         ↓
Expansion of Ischemic Injury
(Microinfarcts, Oligemia)
         ↓
Worse Functional Outcomes
(Higher mRS, More Disability)
```

## 5. Clinical Implications

### 5.1 Implications for Post-EVT BP Management

**Revised Algorithm for BP Targets Post-EVT:**

```
POST-EVT BLOOD PRESSURE MANAGEMENT

Successful Reperfusion (TICI 2b-3)
         ↓
    Check Baseline MRI
    (WMH Assessment)
         ↓
   ┌────┴────┐
   │         │
 Fazekas   Fazekas
   <3        ≥3
   │         │
   ↓         ↓
Can consider  AVOID Intensive BP
intensive BP (<140 mmHg)
if clinically ↓
indicated    Target: 140-180 mmHg
         (or higher if tolerated)
```

### 5.2 Practical BP Targets

| Patient Group | Systolic BP Target | Rationale |
|---------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Severe WMH (Fazekas ≥3)** | **140–180 mmHg** | Avoid harm from hypoperfusion |
| **Mild WMH (Fazekas <3)** | Individualized; may consider <140 mmHg if tolerated | No significant harm demonstrated |
| **No MRI available** | 140–180 mmHg | Cautious approach pending evidence |
| **Hemorrhagic transformation** | <180 mmHg | Balance perfusion vs bleeding risk |
| **Malignant edema** | Individualize; higher targets may be needed | Prevent herniation |

### 5.3 Implications for Stroke Systems of Care

**Prehospital/Emergency Department:**
- Early notification for EVT candidates
- Expedited transfer to comprehensive stroke center
- BP management en route (avoid precipitous lowering)

**Neurointerventional Suite:**
- Periprocedural BP: Maintain MAP to ensure collateral flow
- Post-procedure: Immediate BP assessment and target setting
- Consider WMH burden in BP decision-making when MRI available

**Neurocritical Care/Stroke Unit:**
- Continuous BP monitoring (arterial line)
- Individualized targets based on WMH severity
- Avoid BP variability (U-shaped curve worse outcomes)
- Monitor for signs of hypoperfusion (new deficits, imaging changes)

### 5.4 Imaging Considerations

**When is WMH Assessment Available?**

| Setting | WMH Assessment | Feasibility |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Comprehensive stroke center (with MRI) | Yes | Routine pre-EVT MRI often available |
| Primary stroke center (transfer needed) | Often post-procedure | May not affect acute BP decisions |
| Mobile stroke unit | Limited | CT only typically |

**WMH Assessment Methods Beyond Fazekas:**
| Method | Measurement | Clinical Use |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| **Fazekas scale** | Visual rating 0–6 | Research; clinical practice |
| **Scheltens scale** | Volume-based | Research |
| **WMH volume (automated)** | Quantitative mL | Research |
| **ARWMC scale** | Regional ratings | Research |

## 7. Limitations and Future Directions

### 7.1 Study Limitations

| Limitation | Impact | Mitigation |
|------------|--------|------------|
| **Secondary analysis** | Not pre-specified as primary; hypothesis-generating | Pre-specified subgroup analysis; consistent with biological plausibility |
| **Sample size** | n=271; limited power for subgroups | Adequate for primary outcome; trends consistent |
| **Open-label design** | Potential bias in BP measurement | Blinded endpoint adjudication; objective primary outcome |
| **Single ethnicity** | South Korean population | Generalizability to other ethnicities unknown |
| **MRI timing** | WMH assessed pre-procedure; acute changes possible | Standard clinical practice; acute WMH changes unlikely in 24h |
| **WMH only** | Other CSVD markers not assessed | Fazekas is validated proxy for CSVD burden |

### 7.2 Unanswered Questions

| Question | Current Evidence | Needed Research |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Optimal BP target in severe WMH** | 140–180 mmHg; harm <140 mmHg | RCT with WMH stratification |
| **BP variability** | High variability associated with worse outcomes | Optimal BP stability targets |
| **Dynamic autoregulation** | Static measures only | Continuous autoregulation monitoring |
| **Long-term outcomes** | 90-day only | 1-year functional outcomes |
| **Other CSVD markers** | WMH only | Microbleeds, lacunes, perivascular spaces |
| **Generalizability** | South Korea only | Multi-ethnic validation |

### 7.3 Future Research Directions

1. **Prospective RCT with WMH stratification** — powered to detect difference in severe WMH subgroup
2. **Continuous autoregulation monitoring** — personalized BP targets based on real-time cerebral perfusion
3. **Multimodal CSVD assessment** — combining WMH, microbleeds, lacunes, perivascular spaces
4. **Imaging-based BP individualization** — CT or MRI perfusion to guide BP targets
5. **Long-term follow-up** — 1-5 year outcomes including cognitive function

## 8. Conclusions and Expert Commentary

### 8.1 Summary of Evidence

The secondary analysis of the OPTIMAL-BP trial provides compelling evidence that pre-existing WMH severity significantly modifies the effect of intensive BP lowering post-EVT:

1. **Severe WMH (Fazekas ≥3) patients** receiving intensive BP control (<140 mmHg) had significantly worse functional outcomes (29.9% mRS 0–2) compared to conventional management (51.3%) — a 21.4 percentage point difference

2. **Mild WMH patients** showed no significant difference between intensive and conventional BP management (62.3% vs 68.5%), suggesting the harm is specific to the severely affected population

3. **Significant treatment effect modification** was confirmed (P = .008 for interaction), indicating the observed heterogeneity is not due to chance

4. **Biological plausibility** exists: impaired cerebral autoregulation in severe WMH renders the white matter vulnerable to hypoperfusion when BP is aggressively lowered

5. **Clinical implication:** Uniform intensive BP targets post-EVT are inappropriate; WMH severity should guide individualized BP management

### 8.2 Expert Perspective

> *"This secondary analysis challenges the simplistic 'lower is better' paradigm for post-thrombectomy BP management. The finding that severe WMH identifies a vulnerable population in whom aggressive BP lowering is harmful provides a mechanistic and clinical framework for individualized care."*

> *"Cerebral small vessel disease, as reflected by WMH burden, fundamentally alters the brain's capacity to autoregulate perfusion. In these patients, the 'one size fits all' approach to BP targets fails — and may cause harm."*

> *"This study underscores the importance of incorporating baseline brain health into acute stroke management decisions, much as we would consider age, comorbidities, or stroke severity."*

### 8.3 Take-Home Points for the Practicing Neurologist

1. **Avoid intensive BP lowering (<140 mmHg) in patients with severe WMH** post-EVT — this population showed 21% absolute increase in poor outcomes

2. **Target BP 140–180 mmHg in severe WMH patients** — consistent with current guidelines and the evidence from this analysis

3. **WMH severity modifies treatment effect** — severe WMH identifies patients who will be harmed by intensive BP control

4. **Mild WMH patients** may tolerate intensive BP control without significant harm, but routine intensive targeting is not supported

5. **"One size does not fit all"** — post-EVT BP management must be individualized based on baseline brain health

6. **Obtain baseline MRI when possible** — WMH assessment should be part of acute stroke workup when MRI is available

7. **Current guideline targets (140–180 mmHg)** appear appropriate for most patients, particularly those with severe WMH

8. **Ongoing uncertainty remains** — definitive RCT data with WMH stratification are needed to definitively establish optimal targets
