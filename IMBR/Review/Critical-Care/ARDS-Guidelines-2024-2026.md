## Abstract

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) remains a leading cause of mortality in critically ill patients, with an incidence of approximately 10% of ICU admissions and mortality rates of 27–37%. Over the past decade, the Berlin Definition (2012) served as the gold standard for diagnosis; however, the COVID-19 pandemic exposed critical limitations in its applicability to non-intubated patients and resource-limited settings. The 2024 Global ARDS Definition represents the first major revision in 12 years, introducing three distinct diagnostic categories, accepting lung ultrasound and SpO₂/FiO₂ ratios, and formalizing the recognition of non-intubated ARDS. Simultaneously, the ATS 2024 Guidelines and ESICM 2023 Guidelines have updated recommendations on respiratory support, prone positioning, ECMO, and pharmacotherapy. This review synthesizes key updates across these major publications, with a focus on clinically actionable recommendations and emerging therapeutic horizons including phenotyping and personalized medicine.

**Keywords:** ARDS, Berlin Definition, Global ARDS Definition 2024, HFNO, prone positioning, ECMO, corticosteroids, phenotyping

## 1. Key Changes: Berlin 2012 → Global Definition 2024

The transition from the Berlin Definition (2012) to the 2024 Global ARDS Definition represents the most significant conceptual shift in ARDS diagnosis since the AECC definition in 1994.

| Feature | Berlin Definition (2012) | Global ARDS Definition (2024) |
|---|---|---|
| **Population scope** | Only intubated, ventilated patients | Intubated + **non-intubated** patients + resource-limited settings |
| **Imaging** | Chest X-ray or CT only | **Lung ultrasound added** as accepted modality |
| **Oxygenation assessment** | PaO₂/FiO₂ (PF) ratio with PEEP ≥5 cmH₂O | **SpO₂/FiO₂ (SF) ratio accepted** (if SpO₂ ≤97%); PF with standardized PEEP |
| **HFNO/NIV patients** | Excluded from diagnosis | **Included** — non-intubated ARDS category (HFNO ≥30 L/min or NIV/CPAP ≥5 cmH₂O PEEP) |
| **Resource-limited settings** | Not addressed | **Kigali modification adopted** — no PEEP requirement; SF ≤315 |
| **Severity stratification** | Mild / Moderate / Severe (PF only) | **Three categories** with separate SF ratio cutoffs for each severity level |
| **PaCO₂ / dead space** | Not addressed | Identified as an important future direction |

### New Severity Classification (Intubated ARDS)

| Severity | PaO₂/FiO₂ | SpO₂/FiO₂ |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | 200 < PF ≤ 300 mmHg | 235 < SF ≤ 315 |
| **Moderate** | 100 < PF ≤ 200 mmHg | 148 < SF ≤ 235 |
| **Severe** | PF ≤ 100 mmHg | SF ≤ 148 |

### Non-Intubated ARDS Category
- **Criteria:** SF ≤ 315 (SpO₂ ≤ 97%) with HFNO ≥ 30 L/min OR NIV/CPAP with PEEP ≥ 5 cmH₂O
- **Rationale:** FLORALI trial (2015) demonstrated mortality benefit of HFNO in acute hypoxemic respiratory failure; COVID-19 further highlighted the need to diagnose ARDS before intubation

## 2. Clinical Recommendations

### 2.1 Definition & Diagnosis (Berlin → Global 2024)

The updated definition broadens diagnostic scope while maintaining core pathophysiological principles:

**Changes from Berlin:**
- **Lung ultrasound** formally recognized: bilateral B-lines and/or consolidations satisfy the imaging criterion — superior sensitivity vs. chest X-ray
- **SF ratio** as a non-invasive alternative: linear correlation established (PF 300 ≈ SF 315; PF 200 ≈ SF 235)
- **Non-intubated patients** now included: HFNO ≥30 L/min or NIV/CPAP ≥5 cmH₂O PEEP qualifies for diagnosis
- **Resource-limited adaptation:** Kigali modification — no PEEP/FiO₂ standardization required

**Critical limitation:** PF and SF ratios are influenced by FiO₂, PEEP, cardiac output, hemoglobin, and shunt fraction. A single measurement may not accurately reflect disease severity. Skin pigmentation affects SpO₂ accuracy.

**Under-recognition remains a problem:** LUNG SAFE study showed ~20% of severe ARDS and ~50% of mild ARDS cases were missed by treating clinicians. Early recognition improves adherence to lung-protective ventilation and reduces mortality.

> **Reference:** Ranieri VM et al. *JAMA* 2012;307:2526–33. doi:10.1001/jama.2012.5669

### 2.2 Respiratory Support: HFNO, NIV, and Mechanical Ventilation

#### High-Flow Nasal Oxygen (HFNO)
- **ESICM 2023:** Strongly recommended for acute hypoxemic respiratory failure (non-cardiogenic, non-COPD)
- Provides heated, humidified oxygen with low-level positive airway pressure
- COVID-19 pandemic accelerated global adoption due to ventilator scarcity
- **Key trial:** FLORALI — reduced mortality vs. standard oxygen or NIV in non-hypercapnic AHRF

#### Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV)/CPAP
- ESICM 2023 suggests CPAP may reduce intubation risk in COVID-19-related AHRF
- Important caveat: FiO₂ measurement is unreliable during NIV due to mask leaks and variable inspiratory pressures
- **ATS 2024:** Conditional recommendation for early NMBA in severe ARDS (see below)

#### Mechanical Ventilation — Lung-Protective Strategy
**Strong recommendations (unchanged since ATS 2017, maintained in ATS 2024):**
- **Tidal volume:** 4–8 mL/kg predicted body weight (PBW); start at 6 mL/kg PBW
- **Plateau pressure:** Maintain < 30 cmH₂O
- **Driving pressure (ΔP):** Emerging as an important ventilation parameter — ΔP = Plateau pressure – PEEP; targeting lower ΔP associated with improved outcomes

> **Reference:** ATS/ESICM/SCCM Joint Statement. *Am J Respir Crit Care Med* 2017;209:24–36. doi:10.1164/rccm.201611-2212ST

#### PEEP Strategy
- **ESICM 2023:** Insufficient evidence to recommend for or against higher vs. lower PEEP — no specific PEEP recommendation
- **ATS 2024:** Conditional recommendation for **higher PEEP without lung recruitment maneuvers** in moderate-to-severe ARDS
- Individualized PEEP titration based on FiO₂ requirements, compliance, and driving pressure is the current standard

#### Recruitment Maneuvers
- **ATS 2017 & 2024:** Strong recommendation **against** routine use of prolonged lung recruitment maneuvers (PEEP ≥35 cmH₂O for >60 seconds) in moderate-to-severe ARDS — associated with higher mortality
- **ESICM 2023:** Also recommends against recruitment maneuvers, including brief high-pressure maneuvers

> **Reference:** Fan E et al. *Am J Respir Crit Care Med* 2017;195:1253–63. doi:10.1164/rccm.201611-2212ST

### 2.3 Prone Positioning

Prone positioning remains one of the most effective, low-cost interventions in ARDS management.

**Mechanism:** Redistribution of ventilation to dorsal lung regions, improved ventilation-perfusion matching, reduced ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI), improved secretion drainage.

**Recommendations:**

| Guideline | Duration | Population |
|---|---|---|
| **ATS 2017** | >12 hours/day | Severe ARDS |
| **ATS 2024** | >12 hours/day | Severe ARDS (strong recommendation) |
| **ESICM 2023** | ≥16 hours/day | Mechanically ventilated moderate-severe ARDS |
| **ESICM 2023** | Awake prone positioning | AHRF (non-intubated, both COVID and non-COVID) |

**Evidence basis:**
- PROSEVA trial (2013): Prone positioning >16 h/day reduced 28-day and 90-day mortality in severe ARDS (relative risk reduction ~50%)
- COVID-19 era: Awake prone positioning widely adopted; reduced intubation rates in selected patients
- **ATS 2024 update:** Strong recommendation maintained — prone positioning >12 h/day for severe ARDS

> **Reference:** Guérin C et al. *N Engl J Med* 2013;368:2159–68. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa1214103

### 2.4 Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation (ECMO)

**ATS 2024:** Conditional recommendation for venovenous ECMO (VV-ECMO) in **selected patients with severe ARDS**

**ESICM 2023:** ECMO is recommended for severe ARDS, particularly in **high-volume centers** with appropriate expertise and resources.

**Patient selection criteria for ECMO:**
- Severe hypoxemia: PF ratio <80 mmHg despite optimized ventilatory support, OR
- Hypercapnia with severe acidosis (pH <7.25) despite lung-protective ventilation
- Ventilator driving pressure >15 cmH₂O despite optimization
- Refractory hypoxemia despite prone positioning

**EOLIA Trial:**VV-ECMO in severe ARDS showed a 60-day mortality trend toward reduction (HR 0.76; 95% CI 0.55–1.04; p=0.09). Crossover design and optimized standard care limit definitive conclusions.

**Key considerations:**
- ECMO center volume correlates strongly with outcomes — high-volume centers have significantly lower mortality
- Anticoagulation requirements increase bleeding risk
- Awake ECMO and early mobilization are emerging practices

> **Reference:** Combes A et al. *N Engl J Med* 2018;378:1965–75. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa1800385

### 2.5 Pharmacotherapy

#### Corticosteroids
- **ATS 2024:** Conditional recommendation **for** corticosteroids in ARDS
- **Most consistent evidence:** Dexamethasone 6 mg IV daily for 10 days (RECOVERY trial subset analysis; COVID-19 ARDS)
- **Alternative:** Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day if dexamethasone unavailable
- Mechanism: Anti-inflammatory effect on diffuse alveolar damage phase; reduced cytokine-mediated lung injury
- **Caution:** Hyperglycemia, secondary infections, muscle weakness

#### Neuromuscular Blocking Agents (NMBAs)
- **ATS 2024:** Conditional recommendation for early NMBA (first 48 hours) in **severe ARDS**
- **ESICM 2023:** Not recommended for non-COVID moderate-severe ARDS (may benefit patients at high risk of pneumothorax)
- Rationale: Reduces patient-ventilator dyssynchrony, oxygen consumption, and risk of VILI
- **Risk:** ICU-acquired weakness with prolonged use (>48 hours)

#### Investigational Pharmacotherapy (EMA 2025 Guideline Context)
- **No pharmacological therapy is currently approved specifically for ARDS**
- EMA Guideline 2025 (Rev.2) provides regulatory framework for clinical investigation of new agents
- Standard of care for clinical trials must include: conservative fluid management, prone positioning, lung-protective ventilation
- Key ongoing areas: anti-inflammatory agents, endothelial barrier stabilizers, surfactants, protease inhibitors

> **Reference:** EMA/67201/2025 — Guideline on clinical investigation of medicinal products in the treatment of patients with ARDS (Revision 2, adopted Feb 2025). [Link](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/documents/scientific-guideline/guideline-clinical-investigation-medicinal-products-treatment-patients-acute-respiratory-distress-syndrome-revision-2_en.pdf)

#### Emerging Therapies (Nature 2025 Advances Review)
| Approach | Mechanism | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Mesenchymal stromal cells (MSCs)** | Immunomodulation, tissue repair | Phase I/II trials |
| **Targeted immunotherapy** | Anti-IL-6, anti-TNF biologics | Investigational |
| **Endothelial barrier stabilizers** | Angiopoietin-1, sphingosine analogs | Preclinical/Phase I |
| **Surfactant replacement** | Reduces surface tension | Limited efficacy to date |
| **CRRT in sepsis-related ARDS** | Blood purification | Investigational |

> **Reference:** Ma W et al. *Signal Transduct Target Ther* 2025;10:75. doi:10.1038/s41392-025-02127-9

### 2.6 Phenotyping & Personalized Medicine

ARDS is not a single disease — its heterogeneity is the greatest challenge in developing effective targeted therapies.

#### Subphenotypes Identified by Latent Class Analysis

| Feature | Hyperinflammatory Phenotype | Hypo-inflammatory Phenotype |
|---|---|---|
| **Systemic inflammation** | High IL-6, IFN-γ, TNF-α | Lower inflammatory markers |
| **Biomarkers** | ↑ Angiopoietin-2/1 ratio, ↑ PAI-1 | Lower biomarkers |
| **Clinical features** | More vasopressor use, sepsis, metabolic acidosis | Lower comorbidity burden |
| **Lung morphology** | Non-focal (diffuse) infiltrates | Focal infiltrates |
| **Lung recruitability** | Higher | Lower |
| **Mortality (90-day)** | Significantly higher | Lower |
| **Treatment response** | May respond differently to PEEP, fluids, NMBA | May respond differently |

> **Reference:** Calfee CS et al. *Lancet Respir Med* 2014;2:611–20. doi:10.1016/S2213-2600(14)70097-9

#### Clinical Implications
- **PEEP selection:** High PEEP may benefit hyperinflammatory/non-focal phenotype; low PEEP may be preferable for focal/hypo-inflammatory phenotype
- **Fluid strategy:** Conservative fluid management benefits hyperinflammatory phenotype
- **NMBA response:** Greater benefit in hyperinflammatory phenotype with high driving pressures
- **Current limitation:** Real-time biomarker testing unavailable at bedside; phenotype assignment remains a research tool

#### Future Directions
- AI-based models incorporating chest X-ray and LUS criteria to enhance phenotyping accuracy
- Point-of-care biomarker platforms for bedside phenotype classification
- Integration of phenotyping into future ARDS definition revisions

## 3. Summary — Take-Home Points (10 ข้อ)

1. **The 2024 Global ARDS Definition** is the first major revision in 12 years, introducing three categories (non-intubated, intubated, resource-limited) and accepting lung ultrasound and SF ratio for diagnosis.

2. **HFNO (≥30 L/min)** and **NIV/CPAP (≥5 cmH₂O PEEP)** patients now qualify for ARDS diagnosis — a paradigm shift from Berlin's intubation-only approach.

3. **Lung-protective ventilation** (tidal volume 4–8 mL/kg PBW, plateau pressure <30 cmH₂O) remains the cornerstone of ARDS management — unchanged since 2017.

4. **Higher PEEP without recruitment maneuvers** is conditionally recommended for moderate-to-severe ARDS (ATS 2024); prolonged recruitment maneuvers are strongly discouraged.

5. **Prone positioning ≥12–16 hours/day** reduces mortality in severe ARDS; awake prone positioning is recommended for non-intubated AHRF patients.

6. **VV-ECMO** should be considered in selected patients with severe refractory ARDS, preferentially at high-volume centers with demonstrated expertise.

7. **Corticosteroids** (dexamethasone 6 mg/day for 10 days) are conditionally recommended for ARDS; no other pharmacotherapy has demonstrated survival benefit in large trials.

8. **ARDS subphenotypes** (hyperinflammatory vs. hypo-inflammatory) demonstrate differential treatment responses and outcomes — precision medicine approaches are the future of ARDS management.

9. **No pharmacological agent** is currently approved specifically for ARDS; EMA 2025 guideline provides the regulatory framework for future clinical trials.

10. **Under-recognition of ARDS** remains a critical problem — approximately 20% of severe ARDS cases are missed by treating clinicians, leading to suboptimal lung-protective ventilation and worse outcomes.
