## Abstract

Sepsis remains a global health priority with approximately 49 million cases and 13 million deaths annually worldwide. The Surviving Sepsis Campaign (SSC) 2026 guidelines—jointly published by the Society of Critical Care Medicine (SCCM) and the European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM)—represent a major update from the 2021 edition, comprising 129 graded statements with 46 entirely new recommendations. Key advances include a refined antibiotic timing framework differentiating sepsis with/without shock, replacement of qSOFA with NEWS/NEWS2/MEWS/SIRS for inpatient screening, upgraded recommendations for balanced crystalloids over 0.9% saline, prolonged beta-lactam infusion as a strong recommendation, and mandatory antimicrobial de-escalation when susceptibilities are available. The 2026 guidelines also address post-discharge rehabilitation, long-term outcomes, and explicitly incorporate resource availability and equity considerations. This review summarizes key changes, clinical recommendations across six domains, and take-home points for practicing clinicians.

**Keywords:** sepsis, septic shock, Surviving Sepsis Campaign, guidelines, resuscitation, antibiotics

## 1. Key Changes from 2021 → 2026

The 2026 guidelines introduced substantial modifications reflecting accumulated evidence over five years. The following table summarizes the most clinically significant changes:

| Area | 2021 Recommendation | 2026 Recommendation | Direction |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| **Screening tool** | qSOFA recommended as screening tool | qSOFA NOT recommended as single tool; use NEWS/NEWS2/MEWS/SIRS instead | ⬆️ Major Change |
| **Beta-lactam infusion** | Suggest prolonged infusion | **Recommend** prolonged infusion | Upgrade (Strong) |
| **ABx de-escalation** | Suggest de-escalation when culture available | **Recommend** de-escalation when culture + susceptibility available | Upgrade (Strong) |
| **Fluid type** | Suggest balanced crystalloids over saline | Suggest balanced crystalloids (GRADE unchanged, language refined) | Clarified |
| **Fluid strategy post-resuscitation** | Insufficient evidence | Suggest liberal OR restrictive strategy individualized | New statement |
| **Dynamic fluid assessment** | Suggest dynamic measures | Suggest dynamic measures (reaffirmed) | Clarified |
| **Norepinephrine vs. vasopressin** | Recommend norepinephrine over others | Suggest norepinephrine over vasopressin/angiotensin II | Downgrade (Conditional) |
| **Inotropes in septic shock** | Suggest dobutamine OR epinephrine | Suggest inotropes generically | Reaffirmed |
| **Blood purification** | Suggest against polymyxin B hemoperfusion | Suggest against blood purification therapies broadly | Expanded |
| **Prehospital ABx** | Not addressed | Suggest prehospital ABx when time to evaluation >60 min | New |
| **Corticosteroids** | Suggest IV corticosteroids | Suggest IV corticosteroids (indications clarified) | Downgrade |
| **MAP target elderly** | Not specifically addressed | Suggest MAP 60–65 mmHg in patients ≥65 years | New |
| **HFNC for hypoxemic failure** | Not addressed | Suggest HFNC over conventional O₂ or NIPPV | New |
| **Awake proning** | Not addressed | Suggest awake proning in non-intubated patients | New |
| **Post-sepsis rehabilitation** | Not addressed | Addressed as new domain (long-term outcomes) | New domain |

**Total:** 129 statements (↑ from ~80 in 2021), 46 new statements not previously addressed

## 2. Clinical Recommendations

### 2.1 Diagnosis and Screening

**Screening in hospitalized patients:**
- **Recommend** using **NEWS, NEWS2, MEWS, or SIRS** over qSOFA as a single tool for inpatient sepsis screening *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Sepsis is a clinical diagnosis** and should not be ruled in or ruled out by a single biomarker or diagnostic test
- Insufficient evidence to recommend novel rapid host response diagnostics

**Diagnostic workup:**
- **Recommend** collecting **blood cultures as soon as possible**, ideally before antimicrobial administration *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** measuring **blood lactate** in adults with possible, probable, or definite sepsis or septic shock *(Low certainty)*
- Sepsis and septic shock are **medical emergencies**—treatment should begin immediately

**Prehospital screening:**
- **Suggest** using a standard sepsis screening tool in acutely ill adults en route to hospital by ambulance or flight *(Very low certainty)*

**Hospital-level interventions:**
- **Recommend** that hospitals implement a **performance improvement program** for sepsis, including screening, standard operating procedures, and quality improvement strategies *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Suggest** implementing a "code sepsis" or "sepsis huddle" protocol *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** ICU admission for sepsis patients requiring ICU-level care within **6 hours** *(Low certainty)*

> **Citation:** Evans L, et al. Surviving Sepsis Campaign: International Guidelines 2026. *Intensive Care Med*. 2026. [DOI: 10.1007/s00134-026-08361-1](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00134-026-08361-1)

### 2.2 Initial Resuscitation

**Fluid resuscitation:**
- **Suggest** administering **≥30 mL/kg IV crystalloid** in the first 3 hours in adults with sepsis-induced hypoperfusion or septic shock *(Low certainty)*
- **Recommend** using **crystalloids as first-line fluid** for resuscitation *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Suggest** using **balanced crystalloids** (e.g., lactated Ringer's, PlasmaLyte) **over 0.9% saline** *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Recommend against** using **starches** (hydroxyethyl starch) *(High certainty)*
- **Suggest against** using **gelatin** *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Suggest against** adding supplemental **albumin** to crystalloids *(Moderate certainty)*

**Post-initial resuscitation fluid strategy:**
- For patients who received 30 mL/kg but still have persistent hypoperfusion: **Suggest** either a **liberal OR restrictive fluid strategy** individualized to patient and health system factors *(Low certainty)*

**Fluid assessment:**
- **Suggest** using **dynamic measures** (e.g., passive leg raise, stroke volume variation, echocardiography) to guide fluid resuscitation over physical examination or static measures alone *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** using **serial lactate measurements** to guide resuscitation in sepsis with elevated lactate or septic shock *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** using **capillary refill time** as an adjunct measure of perfusion *(Low certainty)*

**Hemodynamic monitoring:**
- **Suggest** using either **invasive or noninvasive blood pressure monitoring** in septic shock *(Very low certainty)*
- Insufficient evidence to recommend minimally invasive or noninvasive cardiac output monitoring beyond usual care

**MAP targets:**
- **Recommend** initial MAP target of **65 mmHg** over higher targets *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Suggest** MAP **60–65 mmHg** in patients ≥65 years with septic shock *(Low certainty)*

**Vasopressors:**
- **Recommend** **norepinephrine** as first-line vasopressor over dopamine, epinephrine, or selepressin *(High certainty for dopamine; Low for others)*
- **Suggest against** terlipressin *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** norepinephrine over vasopressin or angiotensin II as first-line *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** adding **vasopressin** when norepinephrine doses are escalating *(Moderate certainty)*
- **Suggest** adding **epinephrine** when MAP remains inadequate despite norepinephrine + vasopressin *(Very low certainty)*

**Inotropes:**
- **Suggest** using **inotropes** in septic shock patients with cardiac dysfunction and persistent hypoperfusion despite adequate fluid status and MAP *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest** adding **dobutamine** to norepinephrine or using **epinephrine alone** *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest against** using **levosimendan** *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest against** using **beta-blockers** as treatment for septic shock *(Very low certainty)*

### 2.3 Antibiotic Therapy

The 2026 guidelines introduce a structured **antibiotic timing framework** differentiating patients by presentation:

**Timing (Strongest recommendation — "immediately, ideally within 1 hour"):**
- **Recommend** immediate antimicrobial therapy (ideally within 1 hour) for **septic shock** *(Very low certainty)*
- **Recommend** immediate antimicrobial therapy (ideally within 1 hour) for **probable or definite sepsis without shock** *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest** within 3 hours for **possible sepsis without shock**, after rapid investigation *(Very low certainty)*

**Rapid assessment principle:**
- Clinicians should perform a **rapid assessment** of infectious vs. non-infectious causes in patients with possible sepsis without shock
- **Suggest** deferring antimicrobials in patients with **low likelihood of infection and no shock** while closely monitoring *(Very low certainty)*

**Prehospital antibiotics:**
- **Suggest** administering antimicrobial therapy **in ambulance** for septic shock patients when anticipated time to in-hospital evaluation >60 minutes *(Very low certainty)*

**Empiric antibiotic selection:**
- **Suggest** empirical antimicrobial therapy with coverage for **MDR pathogens** in high-risk patients *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest against** empirical antimicrobial therapy with MDR coverage in **low-risk patients** *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest against** empirical **antifungal therapy** in most patients *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest** an empiric antibiotic regimen **without anaerobic coverage** unless specific risk factors present *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest** using **pathogen-specific rapid diagnostic tests** on a case-by-case basis *(Low certainty)*
- **Suggest against** using **Candida fungal biomarkers** to guide initiation of empiric antifungal therapy *(Low certainty)*

**Beta-lactam administration:**
- **Recommend** **prolonged (extended or continuous) infusion** of beta-lactams for maintenance (after loading dose) over bolus administration *(Moderate certainty)* ← **Upgraded from conditional to strong**

**Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM):**
- **Suggest** using **TDM** on a case-by-case basis in selected patients *(Very low certainty)*

**De-escalation:**
- **Recommend** **de-escalation** of antimicrobial therapy when a confirmed microbiological diagnosis and susceptibility profile is available *(Very low certainty)* ← **Upgraded from conditional to strong**
- **Suggest** de-escalation even when no pathogens are identified on final cultures *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest against** using Candida fungal biomarkers to guide **discontinuation** of empiric antifungal therapy *(Low certainty)*

**Duration:**
- **Suggest** **shorter over longer duration** of antimicrobial therapy in patients with adequate source control *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest** using **procalcitonin AND clinical evaluation** to guide discontinuation when optimal duration is unclear *(Low certainty)*

**Selective digestive decontamination (SDD):**
- **Suggest** using SDD in mechanically ventilated patients in units with **low prevalence of antimicrobial resistance** *(Moderate certainty)*

> **Citation:** Prescott HC, et al. Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2026. *Crit Care Med*. 2026.

### 2.4 Source Control

**Core principle:**
- Adults with sepsis or septic shock should undergo **rapid evaluation for anatomical diagnoses** requiring emergent source control

**Timing:**
- **Suggest** **early source control** over late source control, ideally **within 6 hours** of diagnosis when source control is required *(Very low certainty)*

**Key considerations:**
- Source control interventions should not be delayed for hemodynamic optimization when the source poses imminent risk (e.g., necrotizing fasciitis, septic joint)
- When delay is necessary (e.g., hemodynamic instability requiring resuscitation first), source control should be achieved as soon as safely possible

### 2.5 Adjunctive Therapies

**Corticosteroids:**
- **Suggest** using **IV hydrocortisone** (typically 200 mg/day continuous infusion or intermittent dosing) in adults with septic shock requiring ongoing vasopressor therapy *(Downgraded from prior versions; Very low certainty)*
- Note: Earlier 2021 suggestion was broader; 2026 clarifies focus on refractory shock

**Blood purification:**
- **Suggest against** using **blood purification therapies**, including hemoperfusion (polymyxin B, HA330), high-dose hemofiltration, or plasma exchange *(Low certainty)*
- This broad recommendation expands on the prior narrower recommendation against polymyxin B alone

**Venous thromboembolism prophylaxis:**
- Standard pharmacologic VTE prophylaxis recommended (as per general ICU care); no sepsis-specific changes

**Stress ulcer prophylaxis:**
- Not specifically addressed in 2026 update; institutional protocols apply

**Mechanical ventilation (ARDS/sepsis-associated):**
- **Recommend** **low tidal volume ventilation** (6 mL/kg ideal body weight) over high tidal volume (>10 mL/kg) in sepsis-associated ARDS *(High certainty)*
- **Suggest** tidal volume 6–8 mL/kg IBW in sepsis-associated hypoxemic respiratory failure without ARDS *(Low certainty)*
- For acute hypoxemic respiratory failure:
  - **Suggest** using **HFNC** over conventional oxygen therapy *(Very low certainty)*
  - **Suggest** using **HFNC** over NIPPV as initial therapy *(Low certainty)*
  - **Suggest** using **HFNC** over high flow alternating with NIPPV *(Very low certainty)*
  - **Suggest** a trial of **awake proning** in non-intubated patients *(Very low certainty)*
- **Suggest** titrating FIO₂ to target either higher or lower oxygen levels depending on patient factors and resources *(Low certainty)*

**Midodrine:**
- Insufficient evidence to recommend oral midodrine for septic shock patients with ongoing vasopressor requirements

**Methylene blue:**
- Insufficient evidence to recommend IV methylene blue in refractory septic shock

### 2.6 Ongoing Management and Long-Term Outcomes

**ICU management:**
- **Suggest** admitting sepsis patients requiring ICU to the ICU within 6 hours *(Low certainty)*

**Post-discharge rehabilitation (NEW domain in 2026):**
- The 2026 guidelines explicitly address the **post-acute phase**, reflecting evidence that 30–40% of sepsis survivors experience new physical, cognitive, and mental health problems
- Suggests structured follow-up and rehabilitation targeting:
  - Physical functional recovery
  - Cognitive recovery
  - Mental health (PTSD, anxiety, depression)
  - Reintegration into community

**End-of-life care:**
- Goals of care discussions should occur early and continue throughout the hospital stay
- Palliative care consultation should be available for patients with sepsis who have poor prognosis or treatment-refractory illness
- Treatment limitations should be respected when consistent with patient values and preferences

**Antimicrobial stewardship integration:**
- The guidelines emphasize **antimicrobial stewardship** as a cross-cutting principle throughout all phases of care
- Key elements: appropriate empiric therapy → reassessment → de-escalation → shortest effective duration

## 3. Summary — Take-Home Points

1. **Sepsis is a medical emergency.** Time to treatment remains the single most modifiable factor. The 2026 "1-hour bundle" (or "3-hour framework" for possible sepsis) remains the cornerstone of initial management.

2. **Screening has changed.** qSOFA should NOT be used as a single inpatient screening tool. Use NEWS, NEWS2, MEWS, or SIRS instead.

3. **Antibiotics: faster and smarter.** Antimicrobial therapy should be given immediately (ideally within 1 hour) for septic shock and probable/definite sepsis. For possible sepsis without shock, a structured 3-hour investigation window is now explicitly endorsed. De-escalation when cultures and susceptibilities are available is now a **strong recommendation**.

4. **Fluids: balanced crystalloids preferred.** Use balanced crystalloids (LR/PlasmaLyte) over 0.9% saline for resuscitation. Starches and gelatin are contraindicated. Dynamic measures guide ongoing fluid management.

5. **MAP 65 mmHg is the default initial target.** In patients ≥65 years, 60–65 mmHg is acceptable. Higher targets offer no mortality benefit.

6. **Norepinephrine remains first-line vasopressor.** Add vasopressin when norepinephrine requirements escalate. Add epinephrine for refractory shock.

7. **Prolonged beta-lactam infusion is now a strong recommendation** (upgraded from conditional). Use extended or continuous infusion for maintenance dosing.

8. **Blood purification therapies (hemoperfusion, high-flux hemofiltration, plasma exchange) should NOT be used** — the recommendation was expanded from polymyxin B alone to the entire class.

9. **HFNC and awake proning** are now recommended options for non-intubated patients with hypoxemic respiratory failure.

10. **Post-sepsis care matters.** The 2026 guidelines explicitly address long-term outcomes — survivors need structured follow-up for physical, cognitive, and psychological recovery.
